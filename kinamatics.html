3.1 Motion in a Straight Line

In this unit, you will be introduced to the basic concepts of motion. We encounter motion in our day-to-day activities and have enough experience about it. You might have learned in lower grades that everything in the universe moves. It is because of this that motion is one of the key topics in physics. We use the basic concepts of distance, displacement, speed, velocity, and acceleration to express motion. There are different types of motions. Motion in a straight line is one of the simplest forms of motion in a specific direction. The motions of a car on a road, the motion of a train along a straight railway track, or an object falling freely are examples of one-dimensional motion.

3.1.1 Position, Distance and Displacement

The most convenient example to explain about position, distance, and displacement is your daily travel from your home to your school. When you go to school, your journey begins from your home. Your home is your original position. After some time, you will reach your school. Your school is your final position. In this process, you are continuously changing your position. While traveling from home to school, you are increasing the gap between your present position and your home. This continuous change of position is known as motion. Note that your change of position is observed by considering the distance from your school to home. Your home is taken as a reference frame. Motion is a continuous change in position of an object relative to the position of a fixed object called reference frame.

A body is said to be at rest in a frame of reference when its position in that reference frame does not change with time. If the position of a body changes with time in a frame of reference, the body is said to be in motion in that frame of reference. The concepts of rest and motion are completely relative; a body at rest in one reference frame may be in motion with respect to another reference frame. For example, if you are 2 m from the doorway inside your classroom, then your reference point is the doorway. Your classroom can be used as a reference frame. In the classroom, the walls are not moving, and can be used as a fixed frame of reference. We commonly use the origin as a fixed reference point to describe motion along a straight line.

a. Position

To describe the motion of a particle, we need to be able to describe the position of the particle and how that position changes as the particle moves. Motion is the change in the position of the object with respect to a fixed point as the time passes. For one-dimensional motion, we often choose the x-axis as the line along which the motion takes place. Positions can therefore be negative or positive with respect to the origin of the x-axis. A rider in motion changes its position as it moves.

Have you ever used Google Maps to locate your geographical position while you are moving from some place to another? Google Maps is a Web-based service that provides detailed information about geographical regions and sites around the world. In addition to conventional road maps, Google Maps offers aerial and satellite views of many places. Google Maps provides you with the longitude (east-west position) and latitude (north-south position) coordinates of a location or position of a place.

You can see how far you have travelled and how you travelled from place to place, such as walking, biking, driving or on public transport.

b. Distance

Distance travelled is a measure of the actual distance covered during the motion of a body. In other words, distance is the total path length traveled by the body. The distance travelled does not distinguish between motion in a positive or negative direction. This means that it is a scalar physical quantity (it only has magnitude, not direction). The SI unit of distance is meter (m), though it can also be measured in other non-SI units such as kilometer (km), miles (mi), centimeter (cm), etc. The symbol for distance is \(s\).

Example 1:

A car travels 100 meters north, then turns around and travels 50 meters south. What is the total distance traveled?

Solution:

The distance traveled is the total path length. So, distance = 100 m + 50 m = 150 m.


Example 2:

A person walks 5 km from home to a park, and then walks another 3 km from the park to a cafe. What is the total distance the person walked?

Solution:

The total distance walked is the sum of all path lengths. So, total distance = 5 km + 3 km = 8 km.

c. Displacement

When an object moves, it changes its position. This change of position in a certain direction is known as displacement. A displacement is described by its magnitude and direction. Hence, it is a vector quantity. Displacement is independent of the path length taken. For example, you travel from your home to school. After school, you travel to your home. Therefore, the change in your position when you return back to your home is zero. In this case, we say that your displacement is zero. The SI unit of displacement is the same as the SI unit of distance, which is meter (m).

If a student is at position \(X_i\) at time \(t_i\) and at a later time, \(t_f\), the student is at position \(X_f\), the change in the student's position, \(X_f - X_i\), is called a displacement. Thus, displacement \(\vec{S}\) can be written as

$$ \vec{S} = X_f - X_i $$

Distance Displacement
It is the length of path travelled by an object in a given time. It is the shortest distance between the initial and final positions.
It is a scalar quantity. It is a vector quantity.
It depends on the path followed by the object. It depends on the initial and final positions of the object, but not necessarily on the path followed.
It can be more than or equal to the magnitude of displacement. Its magnitude can be less than or equal to the distance.

Example 1:

A cyclist rides 3 km west and then turns around and rides 2 km east. What is her displacement and the magnitude of her displacement?

Solution:

To solve this problem, we need to find the difference between the final position and the initial position while taking care to note the direction on the axis. Let's choose east as positive and west as negative.

Initial displacement (west): -3 km

Final displacement (east from origin after turning): -3 km + 2 km = -1 km (meaning 1 km west from the starting point)

a) Displacement: The rider's displacement is \(\vec{S} = X_f - X_i = -1 \text{ km}\) or 1 km west.

c) The magnitude of the displacement is 1 km.


Example 2:

A car travels 100 meters north, then turns around and travels 50 meters south. What is the displacement?

Solution:

Let's define north as the positive direction and south as the negative direction.

Initial position \(X_i = 0\) m.

The car travels 100 m north, so its position is \(+100\) m.

Then it travels 50 m south. So, from the 100 m mark, it moves -50 m. The final position \(X_f = 100 \text{ m} - 50 \text{ m} = 50 \text{ m}\).

Displacement \(\vec{S} = X_f - X_i = 50 \text{ m} - 0 \text{ m} = 50 \text{ m}\). So, the displacement is 50 m north.

3.1.2 Average Speed and Instantaneous Speed

Speed is a quantity that describes how fast a body moves. Speed is the rate at which an object changes its location. Like distance, speed is a scalar quantity because it has a magnitude but no direction. Since speed is a rate, it depends on the time interval of motion. Its symbol is \(v\). In other words, speed is the distance covered by a moving body per unit time. The SI unit of speed is meter per second (m/s). Other units of speed include kilometer per hour (km/h) and miles per hour (mi/h). The mathematical equation used to calculate speed is:

$$ \text{speed} = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{time}} $$

$$ v = \frac{s}{t} $$

One of the most obvious features of an object in motion is how fast it is moving. In your journey from home to school, you walk slowly for some time, and you run another time to cover the total distance. This shows that the speed for the walk and the speed for the run are different. In this regard, we define average speed. Speed and average speed are not the same although they are derived from the same formula. The average speed is defined as the total distance travelled divided by the total time it takes to travel that distance:

$$ \text{Average speed} = \frac{\text{Total distance covered}}{\text{Total time taken}} $$

$$ v_{av} = \frac{s_{tot}}{t_{tot}} $$


Example 1:

A car covers a distance between two towns which are 80 km apart. If it takes the car 1hr and 30 minutes to travel between the two towns, calculate the average speed of the car in m/s.

Solution:

The car takes 1hr and 30 minutes to travel between the two towns. This time is the same as 1.5 hrs. Therefore, the average speed of the car is given by,

$$ v_{av} = \frac{s}{t} $$

with \(s = 80 \text{ km}\) and \(t = 1.5 \text{ hrs}\), \(v_{av}\) becomes,

$$ v_{av} = \frac{(80 \text{ km})}{(1.5 \text{ hrs})} = 53.33 \text{ km/hr} $$

However, we are required to calculate the average speed in m/s. For this purpose, we use \(1 \text{ km} = 1000 \text{ m}\) and \(1 \text{ hr} = 3600 \text{ s}\). Hence,

$$ v_{av} = 53.33 \frac{\text{km}}{\text{hr}} = 53.33 \times \frac{1000 \text{ m}}{3600 \text{ s}} = 14.81 \text{ m/s} $$


Example 2:

How far does a student walk in 1.5 hrs if her average speed is 5 m/s?

Solution:

To find the distance, we rewrite the equation \(v = \frac{s}{t}\) as \(s = vt\).

Given: \(v_{av} = 5 \text{ m/s}\), \(t = 1.5 \text{ hrs}\).

First, convert time to seconds: \(1.5 \text{ hrs} = 1.5 \times 3600 \text{ s} = 5400 \text{ s}\).

Now, calculate the distance:

$$ s = v_{av} \times t $$

$$ s = 5 \text{ m/s} \times 5400 \text{ s} $$

$$ s = 27000 \text{ m} $$

So, the student walks 27000 m.

During a typical trip to school by car, the car undergoes a series of changes in its speed. If you were to look at the speedometer readings at regular intervals, you would notice that it changes. The speedometer of a car gives information about the instantaneous speed of the car. It shows the speed of the car at a particular instant in time. The speed at any specific instant is called the instantaneous speed. To calculate the instantaneous speed, we need to consider a very short time interval—one that approaches zero. For example, a school bus undergoes changes in speed. Mathematically the instantaneous speed is given by:

$$ v_{ins} = \frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t} \text{ as } \Delta t \to 0 $$

where, \(\Delta s\) is the distance travelled during the given very short time interval \(\Delta t\). Instantaneous speed and average speed are both scalar quantities. When you solve the average of all instantaneous speeds that occurred during the whole trip, you will get the average speed.

3.1.3 Average Velocity and Instantaneous Velocity

Where an object started and where it stopped does not completely describe the motion of the object. Velocity is a physical quantity that describes how fast a body moves as well as the direction in which it moves. Hence, velocity is a vector quantity. Its symbol is \(\vec{v}\) (v with an arrow on the head). The SI unit of velocity is meter per second (m/s). Other units of velocity include kilometer per hour (km/h) and miles per hour (mi/h).

Suppose that the positions of a car are \(X_i\) at time \(t_i\) and \(X_f\) at time \(t_f\). If the details of the motion at each instant are not important, the rate is usually expressed as the average velocity. Average velocity (\(v_{av}\)) of a body is the total displacement covered by that body in a specified direction divided by the total time taken to cover the displacement. Analytically it can be written as:

$$ \vec{v}_{av} = \frac{\vec{X}_f - \vec{X}_i}{t_f - t_i} = \frac{\Delta \vec{X}}{\Delta t} = \frac{\vec{S}}{\Delta t} $$

where \(\vec{X}_f\) is the final position at final time \(t_f\) and \(\vec{X}_i\) is the initial position at time \(t_i\). Average velocity points in the same direction as the displacement. If the displacement points in the positive x-direction, the average velocity is also in the positive x-direction. If the displacement points in the negative x-direction, the average velocity is in the negative x-direction.

To determine the velocity at some instant, such as \(t = 1.0 \text{ s}\), or \(t = 2.0 \text{ s}\) etc., we study a small time interval near that instant. As the time intervals become smaller and smaller, the average velocity over that interval becomes instantaneous velocity. Instantaneous velocity of a body is its velocity at any time \(t\). For a body that undergoes uniform motion, the velocity of the body is uniform and the average velocity and the instantaneous velocity are the same. Instantaneous velocity can be positive or negative. The magnitude of the instantaneous velocity is known as the instantaneous speed.


Example 1:

A student attained a displacement of 360 m north in 180 s. What was the student's average velocity?

Solution:

We know that the displacement is 360 m north and the time is 180 s. We can use the formula for average velocity to solve the problem:

$$ \vec{v}_{av} = \frac{\Delta \vec{X}}{\Delta t} $$

$$ \vec{v}_{av} = \frac{360 \text{ m North}}{180 \text{ s}} = 2 \frac{\text{m}}{\text{s}} \text{ North} $$

The student's average velocity was 2 m/s North.


Example 2:

A bus moving along a straight line towards west covers the following distances in the given time intervals. Calculate the average velocity of the bus for each time interval.

S in km 0 20 60 100 140
t in hour 0 1 2 3 4

Solution:

By computing the displacement of the bus for each time interval, we can calculate the average velocity of the bus as follows.

Between \(t_0 = 0\) and \(t_1 = 1 \text{ hr}\):

The displacement \(\Delta \vec{X}_1 = X_f - X_i = 20 \text{ km} - 0 \text{ km} = 20 \text{ km}\).

The average velocity during this time interval is:

$$ \vec{v}_1 = \frac{\Delta \vec{X}_1}{\Delta t_1} = \frac{20 \text{ km}}{1 \text{ hr}} = 20 \frac{\text{km}}{\text{hr}} $$

Between \(t_1 = 1 \text{ hr}\) and \(t_2 = 2 \text{ hr}\):

The displacement \(\Delta \vec{X}_2 = X_f - X_i = 60 \text{ km} - 20 \text{ km} = 40 \text{ km}\).

The average velocity of the bus during this time interval is:

$$ \vec{v}_2 = \frac{\Delta \vec{X}_2}{\Delta t_2} = \frac{40 \text{ km}}{1 \text{ hr}} = 40 \frac{\text{km}}{\text{hr}} $$

Between \(t_2 = 2 \text{ hr}\) and \(t_3 = 3 \text{ hr}\):

The displacement \(\Delta \vec{X}_3 = X_f - X_i = 100 \text{ km} - 60 \text{ km} = 40 \text{ km}\).

The average velocity during this time interval is:

$$ \vec{v}_3 = \frac{\Delta \vec{X}_3}{\Delta t_3} = \frac{40 \text{ km}}{1 \text{ hr}} = 40 \frac{\text{km}}{\text{hr}} $$

Between \(t_3 = 3 \text{ hr}\) and \(t_4 = 4 \text{ hr}\):

The displacement \(\Delta \vec{X}_4 = X_f - X_i = 140 \text{ km} - 100 \text{ km} = 40 \text{ km}\).

The average velocity during this time interval is:

$$ \vec{v}_4 = \frac{\Delta \vec{X}_4}{\Delta t_4} = \frac{40 \text{ km}}{1 \text{ hr}} = 40 \frac{\text{km}}{\text{hr}} $$


3.1.4 Acceleration

The discussion of motion with varying velocity can be dealt with by the introduction of the concept of acceleration. Acceleration is a vector quantity and is a measure of how much the velocity of an object changes in a unit of time (in one second). Acceleration is denoted by \(\vec{a}\) and its SI unit is \(\frac{\text{m}}{\text{s}^2}\), that is, meters per second squared or meters per second per second. Acceleration occurs when velocity changes in magnitude (an increase or decrease in speed) or in direction, or both. Acceleration is, therefore, a change in speed or direction, or both.

If the initial velocity of a body is \(\vec{v}_i\) at a time \(t_i\), and the final velocity is \(\vec{v}_f\) at a time \(t_f\), the average acceleration is, from the definition,

$$ \vec{a}_{av} = \frac{\vec{v}_f - \vec{v}_i}{t_f - t_i} $$

If a body starts from rest, then the initial velocity is zero (\(\vec{v}_i = 0\)). If the velocity of a body decreases, then the final velocity is less than the initial velocity. Such motion is called decelerating motion. Deceleration is the negative of acceleration. If the body comes to rest, the final velocity is zero (\(\vec{v}_f = 0\)).


Example 1:

A train moving in the east direction accelerates from rest to 36 km/h in 20 s. What is the average acceleration during that time interval?

Solution:

Given: \(\vec{v}_i = 0\), \(\vec{v}_f = 36 \text{ km/h}\), \(\Delta t = 20 \text{ s}\).

Required: \(\vec{a}_{av} = ?\)

First, convert the final velocity to m/s:

$$ 36 \frac{\text{km}}{\text{h}} = 36 \times \frac{1000 \text{ m}}{3600 \text{ s}} = 10 \frac{\text{m}}{\text{s}} $$

Now, calculate the average acceleration:

$$ \vec{a}_{av} = \frac{\vec{v}_f - \vec{v}_i}{\Delta t} $$

$$ \vec{a}_{av} = \frac{10 \frac{\text{m}}{\text{s}} - 0}{20 \text{ s}} = +0.5 \frac{\text{m}}{\text{s}^2} \text{ to east} $$

The plus sign in the answer means that acceleration is to the right (east). This is a reasonable conclusion because the train starts from rest and ends up with a velocity directed to the right (i.e., positive). So, acceleration is in the same direction as the change in velocity.


Example 2:

A car travelling at 7.0 m/s along a straight road accelerates 2.5 m/s\(^2\) to reach a speed of 12.0 m/s. How long does it take for this acceleration to occur?

Solution:

Given: \(\vec{v}_i = 7.0 \text{ m/s}\), \(\vec{v}_f = 12.0 \text{ m/s}\), \(\vec{a}_{av} = 2.5 \text{ m/s}^2\).

Required: \(\Delta t = ?\)

We use the formula for average acceleration: \(\vec{a}_{av} = \frac{\vec{v}_f - \vec{v}_i}{\Delta t}\). We need to solve for \(\Delta t\):

$$ \Delta t = \frac{\vec{v}_f - \vec{v}_i}{\vec{a}_{av}} $$

$$ \Delta t = \frac{12.0 \text{ m/s} - 7.0 \text{ m/s}}{2.5 \text{ m/s}^2} $$

$$ \Delta t = \frac{5.0 \text{ m/s}}{2.5 \text{ m/s}^2} = 2 \text{ s} $$

It takes 2 seconds for this acceleration to occur.

3.1.5 Uniform Motion

Uniform motion is the motion of an object along a straight line with a constant velocity or speed in a given direction. In a uniform motion, an object travels equal distances in fixed intervals of time. In fact, a moving body does not have a uniform speed throughout its motion. Sometimes the body speeds up or slows down, and other times it moves with a constant speed. This is why describing motion in terms of average quantities (average speed and average velocity) is highly important. Some examples of a uniform motion are a car moving on a straight road with a fixed speed and an airplane flying with constant speed in a given direction.

The uniform rectilinear motion has the following properties:

  • The acceleration is zero (\(a=0\)) because neither the magnitude of the velocity nor its direction changes.
  • On the other hand, the average and instantaneous velocities have the same values at all times.

The position-time graph of a car is a straight line passing through the points (0 s, 0 m) and (10 s, 50 m). Calculate the average velocity of the car.

This indicates that the object is at rest at a position of 20 m from the origin.

c. Speed Limit and Traffic Safety

Have you ever noticed a traffic sign of a speed limit? It indicates that drivers are required to keep the speed of their cars at 80 km/hr or below. Example 1:

A car's velocity-time graph shows a line starting at (0 s, 10 m/s) and ending at (5 s, 30 m/s). Calculate the acceleration of the car.

Solution:

Given two points \((t_1, v_1) = (0 \text{ s}, 10 \text{ m/s})\) and \((t_2, v_2) = (5 \text{ s}, 30 \text{ m/s})\).

The acceleration is the slope of the velocity-time graph:

$$ a = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \frac{v_2 - v_1}{t_2 - t_1} $$

$$ a = \frac{30 \text{ m/s} - 10 \text{ m/s}}{5 \text{ s} - 0 \text{ s}} = \frac{20 \text{ m/s}}{5 \text{ s}} = 4 \text{ m/s}^2 $$

The acceleration of the car is 4 m/s\(^2\).


Example 2:

A velocity-time graph for an object shows a horizontal line at \(v = 15 \text{ m/s}\) from \(t = 0 \text{ s}\) to \(t = 8 \text{ s}\). What is the acceleration of the object, and what is the distance covered during this time?

Solution:

Since the velocity is constant (\(15 \text{ m/s}\)), the change in velocity \(\Delta v = 0\).

The acceleration is \(a = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \frac{0 \text{ m/s}}{8 \text{ s}} = 0 \text{ m/s}^2\).

The distance covered is the area under the v-t graph, which is a rectangle:

$$ \text{Distance} = \text{velocity} \times \text{time} $$

$$ \text{Distance} = 15 \text{ m/s} \times 8 \text{ s} = 120 \text{ m} $$

The acceleration of the object is 0 m/s\(^2\), and the distance covered is 120 m.

3.2 Uniformly Accelerated Motion

3.2.1 Acceleration

When you're in a bus or car, you notice its speed isn't constant. It speeds up, slows down, and turns. Any change in an object's velocity—whether it's speeding up, slowing down, or changing direction—is called acceleration. Acceleration describes the rate at which velocity changes over a given time interval.

i. Average Acceleration

Just like with velocity, we can talk about average acceleration. This is defined as the change in an object's velocity divided by the time interval during which that change occurs.

If an object has an initial velocity \(v_0\) at time \(t_0\) and a final velocity \(v\) at time \(t\), its average acceleration (\(a_{av}\)) is:

\( a_{av} = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \frac{v - v_0}{t - t_0} \)

Here, \(t - t_0\) is the length of the time over which the motion changes. The SI unit for acceleration is meters per second squared (\(\text{m/s}^2\)).

The direction of average acceleration is the same as the direction of the change in velocity. If an object is speeding up, its acceleration is in the same direction as its motion. For example, when you start pedaling a bicycle, its speed increases, and so it accelerates in the direction you're moving.

Conversely, if an object is slowing down, its acceleration is in the opposite direction to its motion. This is often called deceleration. Imagine a train coming into a station and applying its brakes; it decelerates, meaning its acceleration is in the direction opposite to its movement.

Key Concepts

Acceleration occurs whenever an object speeds up, slows down, or changes direction.

When an object speeds up, its final speed is greater than its initial speed, resulting in a positive change in velocity and thus positive acceleration. When an object slows down, its final speed is less than its initial speed, leading to a negative change in velocity and negative acceleration.

Example 1: Calculating Average Acceleration

A car accelerates on a straight road from rest to 75 km/h in 5 s. What is the magnitude of its average acceleration?

Solution:

Given values:

  • Initial velocity \(v_0 = 0 \, \text{m/s}\) (since it starts from rest)
  • Final velocity \(v = 75 \, \text{km/h}\)
  • Time interval \(\Delta t = 5 \, \text{s}\)

First, convert the final velocity from km/h to m/s for consistency with the time unit:

\( v = 75 \, \text{km/h} \times \frac{1000 \, \text{m}}{1 \, \text{km}} \times \frac{1 \, \text{h}}{3600 \, \text{s}} \approx 20.83 \, \text{m/s} \)

Now, use the average acceleration formula: \( a_{av} = \frac{v - v_0}{\Delta t} \)

\( a_{av} = \frac{20.83 \, \text{m/s} - 0 \, \text{m/s}}{5 \, \text{s}} = 4.166 \, \text{m/s}^2 \)

The magnitude of the car's average acceleration is approximately \(4.17 \, \text{m/s}^2\).

Example 2: Automobile Deceleration

An automobile is moving to the right along a straight highway. The driver steps on the brakes. If the initial velocity (when the driver hits the brakes) is 15 m/s and it takes 5.0 s to slow down to 5 m/s, what was the car's average acceleration?

Solution:

Given values:

  • Initial velocity \(v_0 = 15 \, \text{m/s}\) (to the right, so positive)
  • Final velocity \(v = 5 \, \text{m/s}\) (still to the right, so positive, but slower)
  • Time interval \(\Delta t = 5.0 \, \text{s}\)

Using the average acceleration formula: \( a_{av} = \frac{v - v_0}{\Delta t} \)

\( a_{av} = \frac{5 \, \text{m/s} - 15 \, \text{m/s}}{5.0 \, \text{s}} = \frac{-10 \, \text{m/s}}{5.0 \, \text{s}} = -2 \, \text{m/s}^2 \)

The negative sign indicates that the acceleration is in the opposite direction to the car's initial velocity (to the left). This makes sense because the car is slowing down, meaning it is decelerating. Deceleration occurs whenever the magnitude of the velocity is decreasing, and it means velocity and acceleration are in opposite directions.

ii. Instantaneous Acceleration

Similar to instantaneous velocity, instantaneous acceleration refers to the acceleration of an object at a very specific instant in time. While average acceleration gives us a general idea over an interval, instantaneous acceleration provides the exact acceleration at any given moment. For objects undergoing uniform (constant) acceleration, the instantaneous acceleration is the same as the average acceleration.

Key Concepts

Instantaneous acceleration is the average acceleration at a specific instant in time (or over an infinitesimally small time interval).

Mathematically, instantaneous acceleration \( a \) is defined as the change in velocity as the time interval approaches zero:

\( a = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} \)

This concept is particularly important when dealing with motion where acceleration is not constant and changes over time. However, for the purpose of uniformly accelerated motion, we often deal with constant acceleration, making instantaneous and average acceleration equivalent.

3.2.2 Equations of Motion with Constant Acceleration

When an object moves in a straight line, and its velocity changes by equal amounts in equal time intervals, we say it has uniform acceleration. Examples include a bicycle gradually slowing to a stop or a ball dropped from a height. For such motion, we can use a set of equations to relate the object's velocity, acceleration, displacement, and time. These are known as the equations of motion with constant acceleration.

To simplify, we often assume the initial time \(t_0 = 0\) and the initial position \(s_0 = 0\). Let \(v_0\) be the initial velocity and \(v\) be the velocity at any later time \(t\).

i. First Equation of Motion: Velocity-Time Relation

From the definition of average acceleration (\(a_{av} = \frac{v - v_0}{t - t_0}\)), and knowing that for constant acceleration \(a_{av} = a\), we can write:

\( a = \frac{v - v_0}{t - 0} \)

Rearranging this equation gives us the first equation of motion:

\( v = v_0 + at \)

This equation allows us to find the final velocity \(v\) of an object after a time \(t\), given its initial velocity \(v_0\) and constant acceleration \(a\).

ii. Second Equation of Motion: Displacement-Time Relation

When acceleration is constant, the velocity changes linearly with time. In such a case, the average velocity can be calculated as the average of the initial and final velocities:

\( v_{av} = \frac{v_0 + v}{2} \)

We also know that displacement \(s\) is equal to average velocity multiplied by time: \(s = v_{av}t\).

Substituting the expression for \(v_{av}\) into the displacement equation:

\( s = \left(\frac{v_0 + v}{2}\right)t \)

Now, substitute \(v = v_0 + at\) (from the first equation) into this equation:

\( s = \left(\frac{v_0 + (v_0 + at)}{2}\right)t \)

\( s = \left(\frac{2v_0 + at}{2}\right)t \)

Distributing \(t\) and simplifying:

\( s = v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \)

This equation helps us find the displacement \(s\) of an object after time \(t\), given its initial velocity \(v_0\) and constant acceleration \(a\).

iii. Third Equation of Motion: Velocity-Displacement Relation

Sometimes, the time of motion is unknown, and we need an equation that relates velocity, acceleration, and displacement without involving time. We can derive this by combining the first two equations.

From the first equation, \(t = \frac{v - v_0}{a}\).

Substitute this expression for \(t\) into the second equation \(s = \left(\frac{v_0 + v}{2}\right)t\):

\( s = \left(\frac{v_0 + v}{2}\right)\left(\frac{v - v_0}{a}\right) \)

\( s = \frac{v^2 - v_0^2}{2a} \)

Rearranging this equation gives us the third equation of motion:

\( v^2 = v_0^2 + 2as \)

This equation is useful for finding the final velocity \(v\) or displacement \(s\) when time is not known.

Key Concepts

Equations of uniformly accelerated motions are used to solve problems involving constant acceleration.

Example 1: Car Acceleration

A car starts from rest and accelerates uniformly over a time of 5 s for a distance of 100 m. Determine the acceleration of the car.

Solution:

Given values:

  • Initial velocity \(v_0 = 0 \, \text{m/s}\) (starts from rest)
  • Time \(t = 5 \, \text{s}\)
  • Displacement \(s = 100 \, \text{m}\)

We need to find the acceleration \(a\). The equation that relates \(s\), \(v_0\), \(t\), and \(a\) is: \(s = v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\)

Substitute the given values:

\( 100 \, \text{m} = (0 \, \text{m/s})(5 \, \text{s}) + \frac{1}{2}a(5 \, \text{s})^2 \)

\( 100 \, \text{m} = 0 + \frac{1}{2}a(25 \, \text{s}^2) \)

\( 100 \, \text{m} = 12.5a \, \text{s}^2 \)

Solve for \(a\):

\( a = \frac{100 \, \text{m}}{12.5 \, \text{s}^2} = 8 \, \text{m/s}^2 \)

The acceleration of the car is \(8 \, \text{m/s}^2\).

Example 2: Airplane Deceleration

An airplane lands with an initial velocity of 70 m/s and then decelerates at 1.5 m/s\(^2\) for 40 s. What is its final velocity?

Solution:

Given values:

  • Initial velocity \(v_0 = 70 \, \text{m/s}\)
  • Acceleration \(a = -1.5 \, \text{m/s}^2\) (negative because it's decelerating)
  • Time \(t = 40 \, \text{s}\)

We need to find the final velocity \(v\). The equation that relates \(v\), \(v_0\), \(a\), and \(t\) is: \(v = v_0 + at\)

Substitute the given values:

\( v = 70 \, \text{m/s} + (-1.5 \, \text{m/s}^2)(40 \, \text{s}) \)

\( v = 70 \, \text{m/s} - 60 \, \text{m/s} \)

\( v = 10 \, \text{m/s} \)

The final velocity of the airplane is \(10 \, \text{m/s}\). This is less than the initial velocity, as expected when slowing down, but it is still positive, indicating it is still moving forward.

iv. Free Fall

A special case of uniformly accelerated motion is free fall. This is the motion of any object moving solely under the influence of gravity, neglecting air resistance. It's a well-known fact that, in a vacuum, all objects fall towards the Earth with the same constant acceleration, regardless of their mass.

This acceleration due to gravity is denoted by the symbol \(g\). On the Earth's surface, the value of \(g\) is approximately \(9.80 \, \text{m/s}^2\). Its value can vary slightly with altitude and latitude, but for most problems, we use \(9.80 \, \text{m/s}^2\).

Since free fall is essentially one-dimensional motion with constant acceleration, we can use the same equations of motion, with a few modifications:

  • Replace the acceleration \(a\) with \(g\).
  • Replace the displacement \(s\) with vertical height \(h\).

Thus, the equations of motion for free fall become:

\( v = v_0 + gt \)

\( h = v_0t + \frac{1}{2}gt^2 \)

\( v^2 = v_0^2 + 2gh \)

Here, \(v_0\) is the initial velocity, \(h\) is the vertical height, \(g\) is the acceleration due to gravity, and \(t\) is the elapsed time. The direction of \(g\) is always downwards.

Example 1: Falling Mango

A mango fruit has fallen from a tree. Find its velocity and vertical height when it reached the ground if it took 1 s to reach the ground.

Solution:

Given values:

  • Initial velocity \(v_0 = 0 \, \text{m/s}\) (it falls from rest)
  • Acceleration due to gravity \(g = 9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2\)
  • Time \(t = 1 \, \text{s}\)

To find the final velocity \(v\), use the equation: \(v = v_0 + gt\)

\( v = 0 \, \text{m/s} + (9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2)(1 \, \text{s}) = 9.8 \, \text{m/s} \)

To find the vertical height \(h\), use the equation: \(h = v_0t + \frac{1}{2}gt^2\)

\( h = (0 \, \text{m/s})(1 \, \text{s}) + \frac{1}{2}(9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2)(1 \, \text{s})^2 \)

\( h = 0 + \frac{1}{2}(9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2)(1 \, \text{s}^2) = 4.9 \, \text{m} \)

The mango's velocity upon hitting the ground is \(9.8 \, \text{m/s}\), and it fell from a height of \(4.9 \, \text{m}\).

Example 2: Automobile Travel Distance

An automobile starts at rest and speeds up at 3.5 m/s\(^2\) after the traffic light turns green. How far did the automobile travel when it was traveling at 25 m/s?

Solution:

Given values:

  • Initial velocity \(v_0 = 0 \, \text{m/s}\) (starts at rest)
  • Final velocity \(v = 25 \, \text{m/s}\)
  • Acceleration \(a = 3.5 \, \text{m/s}^2\)

We need to find the displacement \(s\). The equation that relates \(v\), \(v_0\), \(a\), and \(s\) is: \(v^2 = v_0^2 + 2as\)

Substitute the given values:

\( (25 \, \text{m/s})^2 = (0 \, \text{m/s})^2 + 2(3.5 \, \text{m/s}^2)s \)

\( 625 \, \text{m}^2/\text{s}^2 = 0 + (7.0 \, \text{m/s}^2)s \)

Solve for \(s\):

\( s = \frac{625 \, \text{m}^2/\text{s}^2}{7.0 \, \text{m/s}^2} \approx 89.3 \, \text{m} \)

The automobile traveled approximately \(89.3 \, \text{m}\).

3.2.3 Graphical Representation of Uniformly Accelerated Motion

Graphs are powerful tools for visualizing and understanding motion. For uniformly accelerated motion, we use three main types of graphs: position-time, velocity-time, and acceleration-time graphs.

i. Position-Time Graph

A position-time graph plots an object's position (\(s\)) on the y-axis against time (\(t\)) on the x-axis. For uniformly accelerated motion, the position-time equation is \(s = v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\).

Since the displacement \(s\) depends on \(t^2\), the graph of position versus time for uniformly accelerated motion is a **parabola** (a curved line). This curve indicates that the velocity is changing over time.

The slope of the tangent line to the position-time graph at any specific point represents the instantaneous velocity at that moment. Because the graph is curved, its slope constantly changes, meaning the instantaneous velocity is also constantly changing.

Key Concepts

The slope of the tangent to the graph of position-time is velocity.

Example 1: Car's Instantaneous Velocity from Position-Time Graph

A car starts from rest and accelerates at 10 m/s\(^2\) for 10 s. Calculate the instantaneous velocity at 4 s using the position-time data.

Solution:

Given \(v_0 = 0 \, \text{m/s}\) and \(a = 10 \, \text{m/s}^2\). We can calculate the position \(s\) at different times \(t\) using \(s = \frac{1}{2}at^2\):

Time (s) 0 1 2 3 4 5 6
Position (m) 0 5 20 45 80 125 180

If we plot this data, we get a parabolic curve. To find the instantaneous velocity at \(t = 4 \, \text{s}\), we need to determine the slope of the tangent line to the curve at that point. From the graph, if we draw a tangent line at \(t=4s\), and pick two points on the tangent line, for example, \((3 \, \text{s}, 40 \, \text{m})\) and \((5 \, \text{s}, 120 \, \text{m})\), the slope is:

\( \text{Slope} = \frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t} = \frac{120 \, \text{m} - 40 \, \text{m}}{5 \, \text{s} - 3 \, \text{s}} = \frac{80 \, \text{m}}{2 \, \text{s}} = 40 \, \text{m/s} \)

Thus, the instantaneous velocity at 4 s is \(40 \, \text{m/s}\).

Example 2: Interpreting Position-Time Graph for Non-Uniform Motion

Imagine a position-time graph for a car that initially accelerates from rest, then moves at a constant velocity, and finally decelerates to a stop. The graph would show:

  • A curved upward-opening parabolic segment during acceleration (slope increasing).
  • A straight, upward-sloping line segment during constant velocity (slope constant).
  • A curved downward-opening parabolic segment during deceleration (slope decreasing, but still positive).

The slope of the tangent at any point on this complex graph would give the instantaneous velocity at that specific moment.

ii. Velocity-Time Graph

A velocity-time graph plots an object's velocity (\(v\)) on the y-axis against time (\(t\)) on the x-axis. For uniformly accelerated motion (constant acceleration), the velocity-time relationship is linear: \(v = v_0 + at\).

This means the velocity-time graph will be a straight line. If the object starts from rest (\(v_0 = 0\)), the line will pass through the origin. If it has an initial velocity (\(v_0 \neq 0\)), the line will start from a point on the y-axis corresponding to \(v_0\).

There are two key pieces of information we can get from a velocity-time graph for uniformly accelerated motion:

  • Area under the velocity-time graph: The area enclosed between the velocity-time graph and the time axis represents the displacement of the object during that time interval. For instance, if the graph forms a rectangle and a triangle, the total area is the sum of the areas of these shapes, which corresponds to the total displacement.

    \(\text{Area} = \text{Displacement} = v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\)

  • Slope of the velocity-time graph: The slope of the straight line on a velocity-time graph represents the acceleration of the object. Since the acceleration is constant, the slope will be constant.

    \(\text{Slope} = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \frac{v - v_0}{t - t_0} = a\)

For uniform retardation (deceleration), the velocity-time graph will still be a straight line, but with a negative slope, indicating that the velocity is decreasing. If the body comes to rest, the line will touch the time axis.

For non-uniform motion (where acceleration is not constant), the velocity-time graph will be a curved line, not a straight one.

Example 1: Total Distance and Displacement from Velocity-Time Graph

Consider a velocity-time graph that shows the following motion:

  • From t=0s to t=4s: velocity increases linearly from 0 m/s to 4 m/s.
  • From t=4s to t=12s: velocity is constant at 4 m/s.
  • From t=12s to t=14s: velocity decreases linearly from 4 m/s to 0 m/s.
  • From t=14s to t=15s: velocity decreases linearly from 0 m/s to -2 m/s.

Calculate the total distance and total displacement after 15 s.

Solution:

We need to find the area under the velocity-time graph, dividing it into geometric shapes:

a) Area for 0s to 4s (Triangle 1):

\( s_1 = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height}\) \(= \frac{1}{2} \times (4 \, \text{s}) \times (4 \, \text{m/s}) = 8 \, \text{m} \)

b) Area for 4s to 12s (Rectangle):

\( s_2 = \text{base} \times \text{height} = (12 \, \text{s} - 4 \, \text{s}) \times (4 \, \text{m/s})\) \(= 8 \, \text{s} \times 4 \, \text{m/s} = 32 \, \text{m} \)

c) Area for 12s to 14s (Triangle 2):

\( s_3 = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height}\) \(= \frac{1}{2} \times (14 \, \text{s} - 12 \, \text{s}) \times (4 \, \text{m/s})\) \(= \frac{1}{2} \times 2 \, \text{s} \times 4 \, \text{m/s} = 4 \, \text{m} \)

d) Area for 14s to 15s (Triangle 3, negative velocity):

\( s_4 = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height}\) \(= \frac{1}{2} \times (15 \, \text{s} - 14 \, \text{s}) \times (-2 \, \text{m/s})\) \(= \frac{1}{2} \times 1 \, \text{s} \times (-2 \, \text{m/s}) = -1 \, \text{m} \)

Total Distance: The sum of the magnitudes of all areas.

\( \text{Total Distance} = |s_1| + |s_2| + |s_3| + |s_4|\) \(= 8 \, \text{m} + 32 \, \text{m} + 4 \, \text{m} + |-1 \, \text{m}| = 45 \, \text{m} \)

Total Displacement: The algebraic sum of all areas.

\( \text{Total Displacement} = s_1 + s_2 + s_3 + s_4\) \(= 8 \, \text{m} + 32 \, \text{m} + 4 \, \text{m} + (-1 \, \text{m}) = 43 \, \text{m} \)

The total distance traveled is 45 m, and the total displacement is 43 m in the positive direction.

Example 2: Analyzing an Airplane's Velocity-Time Graph

An airplane's velocity-time graph shows its velocity increasing linearly from 0 m/s to 30 m/s over the first 3 seconds, then remaining constant at 30 m/s for the next 2 seconds, and finally decreasing linearly to 0 m/s over the last 1 second.

a) What is the airplane's acceleration during the first 3 seconds?

b) What is the airplane's displacement during the entire 6 seconds?

Solution:

a) Acceleration during the first 3 seconds:

During this phase, velocity changes from \(v_0 = 0 \, \text{m/s}\) to \(v = 30 \, \text{m/s}\) over \(\Delta t = 3 \, \text{s}\).

\( a = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \frac{30 \, \text{m/s} - 0 \, \text{m/s}}{3 \, \text{s}} = 10 \, \text{m/s}^2 \)

b) Displacement during the entire 6 seconds:

We'll calculate the area under the graph in three segments:

  • Segment 1 (0s to 3s, triangle): \(s_1 = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height} = \frac{1}{2} \times 3 \, \text{s} \times 30 \, \text{m/s}\) \(= 45 \, \text{m}\)
  • Segment 2 (3s to 5s, rectangle): \(s_2 = \text{base} \times \text{height} = (5 \, \text{s} - 3 \, \text{s}) \times 30 \, \text{m/s}\) \(= 2 \, \text{s} \times 30 \, \text{m/s} = 60 \, \text{m}\)
  • Segment 3 (5s to 6s, triangle): \(s_3 = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height}\) \(= \frac{1}{2} \times (6 \, \text{s} - 5 \, \text{s}) \times 30 \, \text{m/s}\) \(= \frac{1}{2} \times 1 \, \text{s} \times 30 \, \text{m/s} = 15 \, \text{m}\)

Total displacement = \(s_1 + s_2 + s_3 = 45 \, \text{m} + 60 \, \text{m} + 15 \, \text{m} = 120 \, \text{m}\)

iii. Acceleration-Time Graph

An acceleration-time graph plots an object's acceleration (\(a\)) on the y-axis against time (\(t\)) on the x-axis. For uniformly accelerated motion, acceleration is constant over time. Therefore, the acceleration-time graph is a straight line parallel to the time axis.

The area under the acceleration-time graph represents the change in velocity during that time interval. Since the graph is a rectangle (base is time, height is acceleration), the area is simply acceleration multiplied by time.

\(\text{Area} = a \times \Delta t = \Delta v\)

Example 1: Change in Velocity from Acceleration-Time Graph

For an acceleration-time graph showing a constant acceleration of 4 m/s\(^2\) for a duration of 9 seconds, find the change in velocity of the object.

Solution:

Given values:

  • Acceleration \(a = 4 \, \text{m/s}^2\)
  • Time interval \(\Delta t = 9 \, \text{s}\)

The change in velocity is the area under the acceleration-time graph, which is a rectangle in this case:

\( \Delta v = \text{Area} = \text{base} \times \text{height} = 9 \, \text{s} \times 4 \, \text{m/s}^2\) \(= 36 \, \text{m/s} \)

The change in velocity of the moving object is \(36 \, \text{m/s}\).

Example 2: Interpreting an Acceleration-Time Graph

Imagine an acceleration-time graph that shows:

  • A constant positive acceleration of \(5 \, \text{m/s}^2\) for the first 2 seconds.
  • Zero acceleration (a horizontal line on the time axis) for the next 3 seconds.
  • A constant negative acceleration of \(-3 \, \text{m/s}^2\) for the final 1 second.

To find the total change in velocity, we would sum the areas:

  • \(\Delta v_1 = (5 \, \text{m/s}^2) \times (2 \, \text{s}) = 10 \, \text{m/s}\)
  • \(\Delta v_2 = (0 \, \text{m/s}^2) \times (3 \, \text{s}) = 0 \, \text{m/s}\)
  • \(\Delta v_3 = (-3 \, \text{m/s}^2) \times (1 \, \text{s}) = -3 \, \text{m/s}\)

Total change in velocity = \(10 \, \text{m/s} + 0 \, \text{m/s} + (-3 \, \text{m/s}) = 7 \, \text{m/s}\).

3.2.4 Relative Velocity in One Dimension

When we talk about the velocity of an object, it's always implied that we are measuring it with respect to a specific reference frame, or observer. For instance, if you're traveling in a train moving at 100 km/h, another passenger sitting next to you would say your speed is zero relative to them. However, someone standing outside the train on the ground would observe you moving at 100 km/h. This concept, where an object's motion depends on the observer's frame of reference, is called relative motion, and the velocity measured is relative velocity.

Key Concepts

Relative velocity is the velocity of an object with respect to another object or observer.

Let's consider two objects, A and B, moving in one dimension. Let \(v_A\) be the velocity of object A with respect to the ground, and \(v_B\) be the velocity of object B with respect to the ground.

The relative velocity of A with respect to B (\(v_{AB}\)) is given by:

\( v_{AB} = v_A - v_B \)

The relative velocity of B with respect to A (\(v_{BA}\)) is given by:

\( v_{BA} = v_B - v_A \)

In one-dimensional motion, objects can move in the same direction or in opposite directions. It's crucial to assign positive and negative signs consistently to velocities based on a chosen direction.

i. Objects Moving in the Same Direction

If two objects are moving in the same direction, say both to the right (positive), then their relative velocity is the difference between their velocities. For example, if car A is moving at \(v_A = 100 \, \text{km/h}\) and car B is moving at \(v_B = 100 \, \text{km/h}\) in the same direction:

\( v_{AB} = v_A - v_B = 100 \, \text{km/h} - 100 \, \text{km/h} = 0 \, \text{km/h} \)

\( v_{BA} = v_B - v_A = 100 \, \text{km/h} - 100 \, \text{km/h} = 0 \, \text{km/h} \)

This means that with respect to each other, they appear to be at rest, even though both are moving at 100 km/h relative to the ground.

ii. Objects Moving in Opposite Directions

If two objects are moving in opposite directions, one velocity will be positive, and the other negative. For example, if car A moves right at \(v_A = 100 \, \text{km/h}\) and car B moves left at \(v_B = -100 \, \text{km/h}\):

\( v_{AB} = v_A - v_B = 100 \, \text{km/h} - (-100 \, \text{km/h})\) \(= 100 \, \text{km/h} + 100 \, \text{km/h} = 200 \, \text{km/h} \)

\( v_{BA} = v_B - v_A = -100 \, \text{km/h} - 100 \, \text{km/h}\) \(= -200 \, \text{km/h} \)

In this case, the magnitude of their relative velocity is the sum of their individual speeds. Car A perceives car B approaching at 200 km/h, and car B perceives car A approaching at 200 km/h.

Example 1: Motorcycle Overtaking a Car

A motorcycle traveling on the highway at a velocity of 120 km/h passes a car traveling at a velocity of 90 km/h in the same direction. From the point of view of a passenger on the car, what is the velocity of the motorcycle?

Solution:

Let's define the direction of travel as positive.

  • Velocity of motorcycle (\(v_M\)) = \(120 \, \text{km/h}\)
  • Velocity of car (\(v_C\)) = \(90 \, \text{km/h}\)

We want to find the velocity of the motorcycle relative to the car (\(v_{MC}\)):

\( v_{MC} = v_M - v_C \)

\( v_{MC} = 120 \, \text{km/h} - 90 \, \text{km/h} = 30 \, \text{km/h} \)

From the car passenger's perspective, the motorcycle is moving forward at \(30 \, \text{km/h}\).

Example 2: Approaching Automobile and Truck

An automobile is moving at 80 km/h, and a truck is moving at 60 km/h, approaching the automobile from the opposite direction. What is the relative velocity of the automobile with respect to the truck when the observer on the automobile measures it?

Solution:

Let's define the direction of the automobile as positive. So, the automobile is moving in the positive direction.

  • Velocity of automobile (\(v_A\)) = \(80 \, \text{km/h}\)
  • Velocity of truck (\(v_T\)) = \(-60 \, \text{km/h}\) (negative because it's approaching from the opposite direction)

We want to find the velocity of the automobile relative to the truck (\(v_{AT}\)):

\( v_{AT} = v_A - v_T \)

\( v_{AT} = 80 \, \text{km/h} - (-60 \, \text{km/h}) \)

\( v_{AT} = 80 \, \text{km/h} + 60 \, \text{km/h} = 140 \, \text{km/h} \)

From the truck driver's perspective (or an observer on the truck), the automobile is approaching at \(140 \, \text{km/h}\).

3.3 Motion in One and Two Dimensions

Motion is a fundamental concept in physics, observed everywhere in the universe. From cars on roads to airplanes in the sky, and even tiny ants crawling, everything is constantly moving. An object is considered to be in motion if its position changes over time.

The idea of motion is relative. An object can be in motion relative to one reference point but at rest relative to another. For instance, while you sit in a classroom, you are at rest compared to the Earth, but you are moving relative to the Sun.

In this unit, we will explore motion in one dimension with respect to a fixed reference frame and its real-world applications. Additionally, we'll delve into uniform circular motion as an example of two-dimensional motion.

3.3.1 Uniformly Accelerated Motion in 1D

An object is said to accelerate when its velocity changes, either in magnitude, direction, or both. Acceleration is a vector quantity, meaning it has both magnitude and direction. It is defined as the rate of change of velocity or the change in velocity per unit time. The SI unit of acceleration is meters per second squared (\(m/s^2\)).

It's important to understand that negative acceleration doesn't always mean slowing down, and positive acceleration doesn't always mean speeding up. If velocity and acceleration have the same sign (both positive or both negative), the object is speeding up. If they have different signs, the object is slowing down.

Average Acceleration

The average acceleration (\(a_{av}\)) of a particle is the ratio of the change in velocity (\(\Delta\vec{v} = \vec{v}_f - \vec{v}_i\)) to the time interval (\(\Delta t = t_f - t_i\)).

Mathematically, it's expressed as:

\(a_{av} = \frac{\vec{v}_f - \vec{v}_i}{\Delta t}\)

Where \(\vec{v}_f\) is the final velocity, \(\vec{v}_i\) is the initial velocity, and \(\Delta t\) is the time interval. The SI unit of acceleration is \(m/s^2\) or \(ms^{-2}\).

Key Term

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity. It is a vector quantity with a unit of \(m/s^2\).

Example 1:

If a car increases its speed from rest to 20 m/s in 6 seconds, what is its average acceleration?

Solution:
Given: Initial velocity (\(v_i\)) = 0 m/s (from rest), Final velocity (\(v_f\)) = 20 m/s, Time (\(t\)) = 6 s.

Using the formula for average acceleration:

\(a_{av} = \frac{v_f - v_i}{t}\)

\(a_{av} = \frac{20\,m/s - 0\,m/s}{6\,s}\)

\(a_{av} = 3.33\,m/s^2\)

The average acceleration of the car is \(3.33\,m/s^2\).

Example 2:

A car travelling at 15 m/s accelerates to 25 m/s in 5 seconds. What is its average acceleration?

Solution:
Given: Initial velocity (\(v_i\)) = 15 m/s, Final velocity (\(v_f\)) = 25 m/s, Time (\(t\)) = 5 s.

Using the formula for average acceleration:

\(a_{av} = \frac{v_f - v_i}{t}\)

\(a_{av} = \frac{25\,m/s - 15\,m/s}{5\,s}\)

\(a_{av} = \frac{10\,m/s}{5\,s}\)

\(a_{av} = 2\,m/s^2\)

The average acceleration of the car is \(2\,m/s^2\).

Instantaneous Acceleration

Instantaneous acceleration (\(a_{inst}\)) is the acceleration at a specific moment in time. It is found by calculating the average acceleration over a very small time interval (\(\Delta t\)) that approaches zero. This concept is similar to how instantaneous velocity is determined.

\(a = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}\)

Example 1:

The velocity of a particle moving along a straight line is given by the equation \(v(t) = t^2 - 2t\). Find the acceleration of the particle at \(t = 2\,s\).

Solution:
To find the acceleration at any time \(t\), we use the definition of instantaneous acceleration:

\(a(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{v(t+\Delta t) - v(t)}{\Delta t}\)

First, find \(v(t+\Delta t)\):

\(v(t+\Delta t) = (t+\Delta t)^2 - 2(t+\Delta t)\)

\(v(t+\Delta t) = t^2 + 2t\Delta t + (\Delta t)^2 - 2t - 2\Delta t\)

Now, substitute into the acceleration formula:

\(a(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{(t^2 + 2t\Delta t + (\Delta t)^2 - 2t - 2\Delta t) - (t^2 - 2t)}{\Delta t}\)

\(a(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{2t\Delta t + (\Delta t)^2 - 2\Delta t}{\Delta t}\)

\(a(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} (2t + \Delta t - 2)\)

As \(\Delta t \to 0\):

\(a(t) = 2t - 2\)

Now, substitute \(t = 2\,s\) into the acceleration expression:

\(a(2) = 2(2) - 2\)

\(a(2) = 4 - 2\)

\(a(2) = 2\,m/s^2\)

The acceleration of the particle at \(t = 2\,s\) is \(2\,m/s^2\).

Example 2:

A particle's velocity is given by \(v(t) = 3t^2 + 4t\). Find the instantaneous acceleration at \(t = 1\,s\).

Solution:
To find the instantaneous acceleration, we need the derivative of the velocity function with respect to time:

\(a(t) = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt}(3t^2 + 4t)\)

\(a(t) = 6t + 4\)

Now, substitute \(t = 1\,s\):

\(a(1) = 6(1) + 4\)

\(a(1) = 10\,m/s^2\)

The instantaneous acceleration at \(t = 1\,s\) is \(10\,m/s^2\).

Motion with Constant Acceleration

While acceleration can vary in reality, many common types of one-dimensional motion involve constant acceleration (or uniform acceleration). This means the object's velocity changes by the same amount in equal time intervals. This type of motion is also known as uniformly accelerated rectilinear motion. In such cases, the average acceleration is equal to the instantaneous acceleration.

The average velocity in motion with constant acceleration is simply the average of the initial and final velocities:

\(v_{av} = \frac{v_i + v_f}{2}\)

3.3.2 Equations of Uniformly Accelerated Motion in 1D

These equations are essential tools for solving problems involving motion with constant acceleration in a straight line. They are vector equations, meaning the direction of quantities must be considered: add if in the same direction, subtract if in opposite directions.

Derivation of Equations of Motion for Uniformly Accelerated Motion in a Straight Line

The symbols used have the following meanings:

  • \(s\) = displacement
  • \(v_i\) = initial velocity
  • \(v_f\) = final velocity
  • \(t\) = time for the acceleration
  • \(a\) = acceleration

i. Equation 1: From the definition of acceleration

We know that acceleration is the rate of change of velocity:

\(a = \frac{v_f - v_i}{t}\)

Rearranging this equation to solve for final velocity gives:

\(v_f = v_i + at\)

This equation relates final velocity, initial velocity, acceleration, and time.

ii. Equation 2: From the definition of average velocity for constant acceleration

For uniformly accelerated motion, the average velocity is:

\(v_{av} = \frac{v_i + v_f}{2}\)

We also know that displacement is average velocity multiplied by time:

\(s = v_{av}t\)

Substituting the expression for \(v_{av}\):

\(s = \left(\frac{v_i + v_f}{2}\right)t\)

This equation connects displacement, initial velocity, final velocity, and time.

iii. Equation 3: Combining Equation 1 and Equation 2 (deriving without \(v_f\))

Substitute \(v_f = v_i + at\) (from Equation 1) into \(s = \left(\frac{v_i + v_f}{2}\right)t\):

\(s = \left(\frac{v_i + (v_i + at)}{2}\right)t\)

\(s = \left(\frac{2v_i + at}{2}\right)t\)

\(s = v_it + \frac{1}{2}at^2\)

This equation allows us to find displacement without knowing the final velocity.

iv. Equation 4: Combining Equation 1 and Equation 2 (deriving without \(v_i\))

From Equation 1, \(v_i = v_f - at\). Substitute this into \(s = \left(\frac{v_i + v_f}{2}\right)t\):

\(s = \left(\frac{(v_f - at) + v_f}{2}\right)t\)

\(s = \left(\frac{2v_f - at}{2}\right)t\)

\(s = v_ft - \frac{1}{2}at^2\)

This equation allows us to find displacement without knowing the initial velocity.

v. Equation 5: Combining Equation 1 and Equation 2 (deriving without \(t\))

From Equation 1, \(t = \frac{v_f - v_i}{a}\). Substitute this into \(s = \left(\frac{v_i + v_f}{2}\right)t\):

\(s = \left(\frac{v_i + v_f}{2}\right)\left(\frac{v_f - v_i}{a}\right)\)

\(s = \frac{v_f^2 - v_i^2}{2a}\)

Rearranging this gives:

\(v_f^2 = v_i^2 + 2as\)

This powerful equation allows us to find the final velocity (or initial velocity or displacement) without knowing the time.

\(s = v_ft - \frac{1}{2}at^2\) Initial velocity (\(v_i\))

Example 1:

A car accelerates uniformly from rest to a speed of 72 km/h in 8.0 s. (a) Find the acceleration of the car. (b) Find the distance the car travels. (c) If the car then brakes to a stop over a distance of 50 m, find its uniform deceleration.

Solution:
First, convert units: 72 km/h = \(72 \times \frac{1000\,m}{3600\,s} = 20\,m/s\).

(a) Find the acceleration:
Given: \(v_i = 0\,m/s\), \(v_f = 20\,m/s\), \(t = 8.0\,s\).

Using the equation \(v_f = v_i + at\):

\(a = \frac{v_f - v_i}{t}\)

\(a = \frac{20\,m/s - 0\,m/s}{8.0\,s}\)

\(a = 2.5\,m/s^2\)

(b) Find the distance travelled:
Using the equation \(s = v_it + \frac{1}{2}at^2\):

\(s = (0\,m/s)(8.0\,s) + \frac{1}{2}(2.5\,m/s^2)(8.0\,s)^2\)

\(s = 0 + \frac{1}{2}(2.5\,m/s^2)(64\,s^2)\)

\(s = 1.25\,m/s^2 \times 64\,s^2\)

\(s = 80\,m\)

Alternatively, using \(s = \left(\frac{v_i + v_f}{2}\right)t\):

\(s = \left(\frac{0\,m/s + 20\,m/s}{2}\right)(8.0\,s)\)

\(s = (10\,m/s)(8.0\,s)\)

\(s = 80\,m\)

(c) Find the deceleration when braking:
Given: Initial velocity for braking (\(v_i\)) = 20 m/s, Final velocity (\(v_f\)) = 0 m/s, Displacement (\(s\)) = 50 m.

Using the equation \(v_f^2 = v_i^2 + 2as\):

\(0^2 = (20\,m/s)^2 + 2a(50\,m)\)

\(0 = 400\,m^2/s^2 + 100a\,m\)

\(100a\,m = -400\,m^2/s^2\)

\(a = \frac{-400\,m^2/s^2}{100\,m}\)

\(a = -4\,m/s^2\)

The uniform deceleration is \(4\,m/s^2\). The negative sign indicates that the car is slowing down.

Example 2:

A car starts from rest with an acceleration of \(3\,m/s^2\). What is its velocity after 4 seconds, and how far has it traveled?

Solution:
Given: \(v_i = 0\,m/s\) (starts from rest), \(a = 3\,m/s^2\), \(t = 4\,s\).

(a) Velocity after 4 seconds:
Using the equation \(v_f = v_i + at\):

\(v_f = 0\,m/s + (3\,m/s^2)(4\,s)\)

\(v_f = 12\,m/s\)

(b) Distance traveled:
Using the equation \(s = v_it + \frac{1}{2}at^2\):

\(s = (0\,m/s)(4\,s) + \frac{1}{2}(3\,m/s^2)(4\,s)^2\)

\(s = 0 + \frac{1}{2}(3\,m/s^2)(16\,s^2)\)

\(s = \frac{1}{2}(48\,m)\)

\(s = 24\,m\)

After 4 seconds, the car's velocity is \(12\,m/s\) and it has traveled a distance of \(24\,m\).

Not all negative accelerations are decelerations. Negative acceleration can also occur due to a change in direction with respect to a certain fixed reference point.

Determining the Stopping Distance of a Vehicle

When a vehicle applies brakes, the distance it travels before coming to a complete stop is called the stopping distance. Calculating stopping distance is crucial for preventing hazardous situations. Several factors influence stopping distance:

  • Speed of the vehicle: Higher speeds require longer stopping distances.
  • Weight of the vehicle: Heavier vehicles generally need more distance to stop.
  • Road conditions: Slippery surfaces (ice, snow, wet roads) drastically increase stopping distance compared to dry roads.
  • Vehicle brake conditions: Worn brake pads or rotors reduce braking effectiveness.
  • Braking technology: Advanced braking systems can reduce stopping distances.
  • Tire conditions: Worn or improperly inflated tires reduce grip and increase stopping distance.

The stopping distance is the sum of two main components:

i. Reaction Distance: This is the distance the vehicle travels from the moment the driver detects a hazard until they begin to brake or swerve. It depends on the driver's reaction time and the vehicle's speed.

\(Reaction\,Distance = Reaction\,Time \times Speed\)

\(s_r = vt\)

ii. Braking Distance: This is the distance the vehicle travels from the moment the brakes are applied until it comes to a complete stop. This distance is influenced by the initial velocity (\(v\)) and the coefficient of friction (\(\mu\)) between the tires and the road, as the acceleration due to friction is \(a = \mu g\).

\(Braking\,distance\,(s_{break}) = \frac{v^2}{2a} = \frac{v^2}{2\mu g}\)

Therefore, the total stopping distance is:

\(Stopping\,distance\) \(= Reaction\,distance + Braking\,distance\)

\(Stopping\,distance = vt + \frac{v^2}{2\mu g}\)

(a) Length of runway needed (displacement \(s\)):

\(0^2 = (40.0\,m/s)^2 + 2(-1.50\,m/s^2)s\)

\(0 = 1600\,m^2/s^2 - 3.00\,m/s^2 s\)

\(3.00\,m/s^2 s = 1600\,m^2/s^2\)

Example 2:

(a) Compare the stopping distance for a car moving at 80 km/h and 20 km/h on a dry road with a coefficient of friction of 0.70. (b) Discuss the potential damage caused by the automobile running at 80 km/h compared to one moving at 20 km/h.

Solution:
Assume reaction time \(t_{reaction} = 0.7\,s\) (a common estimate) and \(g = 9.8\,m/s^2\). First, convert speeds to m/s:

  • 80 km/h = \(80 \times \frac{1000}{3600} \approx 22.22\,m/s\)
  • 20 km/h = \(20 \times \frac{1000}{3600} \approx 5.56\,m/s\)

(a) Calculate stopping distances:

For 80 km/h (\(v = 22.22\,m/s\)):
Reaction distance (\(s_r\)): \(s_r = vt = (22.22\,m/s)(0.7\,s) \approx 15.55\,m\)

Braking distance (\(s_b\)): \(s_b = \frac{v^2}{2\mu g} = \frac{(22.22\,m/s)^2}{2(0.70)(9.8\,m/s^2)} = \frac{493.73}{13.72} \approx 35.99\,m\)

Total stopping distance (\(S\)): \(S = s_r + s_b = 15.55\,m + 35.99\,m \approx 51.54\,m\)

For 20 km/h (\(v = 5.56\,m/s\)):
Reaction distance (\(s_r\)): \(s_r = vt = (5.56\,m/s)(0.7\,s) \approx 3.89\,m\)

Braking distance (\(s_b\)): \(s_b = \frac{v^2}{2\mu g} = \frac{(5.56\,m/s)^2}{2(0.70)(9.8\,m/s^2)} = \frac{30.91}{13.72} \approx 2.25\,m\)

Total stopping distance (\(S\)): \(S = s_r + s_b = 3.89\,m + 2.25\,m \approx 6.14\,m\)

Comparison: The stopping distance at 80 km/h (\(\approx 51.54\,m\)) is significantly greater than at 20 km/h (\(\approx 6.14\,m\)). This highlights the critical importance of speed limits.

An automobile moving at 80 km/h has approximately 16 times more kinetic energy than one moving at 20 km/h. This means that in a collision, the car moving at 80 km/h would cause significantly more damage, both to itself and to any other object involved, due to the much higher energy that needs to be absorbed or dissipated.

a. Displacement-Time Graphs

A displacement-time graph illustrates how an object's displacement changes over time. When drawing such a graph, displacement (the dependent variable) is plotted on the Y-axis, and time (the independent variable) is plotted on the X-axis.

\(Slope = \frac{rise}{run} = \frac{Vertical\,increase}{Horizontal\,increase} = \frac{x_2 - x_1}{t_2 - t_1}\)

This expression is equivalent to the definition of velocity: \(velocity = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}\).

3.3.4 Vertical Motion (Freely Falling Bodies)

Vertical motion refers to the movement of objects either upwards or downwards. When an object moves vertically solely under the influence of gravity (neglecting air resistance), it is said to be in free fall. The acceleration experienced by a freely falling body is constant and is known as the acceleration due to gravity, denoted by the letter \(g\).

Near the Earth's surface, the magnitude of \(g\) is approximately \(9.8\,m/s^2\). Therefore, the equations of motion with constant acceleration can be applied to free fall, with \(a\) replaced by \(g\), keeping careful track of directions.

Equations for Freely Falling Bodies:

Let's define the upward direction as positive and downward as negative. Since gravity always acts downwards, \(g\) will be negative if upward is positive, and positive if downward is positive. Here, we'll assume upward is positive, making the acceleration due to gravity \(-g\).

Using \(a = -g\), the kinematic equations become:

1. Velocity-Time Equation:
From \(v_f = v_i + at\):

\(v = u - gt\)

Where \(u\) is the initial velocity.

2. Displacement-Time Equation:
From \(s = v_it + \frac{1}{2}at^2\):

\(y = ut - \frac{1}{2}gt^2\)

Where \(y\) is the vertical displacement (height above the origin).

3. Velocity-Displacement Equation:
From \(v_f^2 = v_i^2 + 2as\):

\(v^2 = u^2 - 2gy\)

If an object is thrown vertically downward, both initial velocity (\(u\)) and gravitational acceleration (\(g\)) point downwards. If we define downward as positive, the equations become:

  • \(v = u + gt\)
  • \(y = ut + \frac{1}{2}gt^2\)
  • \(v^2 = u^2 + 2gy\)

3.3.5 Uniform Circular Motion

Uniform circular motion is a special type of curvilinear motion where an object travels in a circle at a constant speed. However, even though the speed is constant, the object's direction of motion continuously changes. Because velocity is a vector (having both magnitude and direction), a change in direction means a change in velocity, and thus, the object is accelerating.

Common examples include a mass tied to a string and swung in a horizontal circle, the hands of a watch, a rotating turntable, and any point on a spinning propeller.

Learning Competencies:

  • Define uniform circular motion.
  • Calculate angular and tangential displacement, angular and tangential velocity, and centripetal acceleration.
  • Explain uniform circular motion in the horizontal plane.
  • Identify the force required for circular motion to take place.

a. Angular and Tangential Displacement

i. Angular Displacement (\(\theta\)):
When an object moves in a circular path, the angle swept by the radius of the circle is called its angular displacement. It is a vector quantity, having a direction (clockwise or anticlockwise). The SI unit for angular displacement is the radian.

If \(s\) is the arc length traveled and \(r\) is the radius of the circle, then:

\(\theta = \frac{s}{r}\)

After one complete revolution, the object travels a distance equal to the circumference of the circle, \(2\pi r\). Thus, the angular displacement for one revolution is:

\(\theta = \frac{2\pi r}{r} = 2\pi\,radians\)

Therefore, \(1\,revolution = 2\pi\,radians = 360^\circ\).

ii. Tangential Displacement (\(s\)):
Tangential displacement is the actual distance covered by the rotating object along the curved path (circumference) of the circle.

b. Angular Velocity and Tangential Velocity

i. Angular Velocity (\(\omega\)):
Angular velocity measures the rate at which angular displacement changes. It is denoted by the Greek letter omega (\(\omega\)) and its standard SI unit is radians per second (\(rad/s\)).

\( \omega = \frac{\Delta \theta}{\Delta t} \)

Angular velocity is also a vector quantity. Its direction is determined by the right-hand rule: if you curl the fingers of your right hand in the direction of the rotating body's motion, your thumb will point in the direction of the angular velocity.

ii. Tangential Velocity (\(v_t\)):
Tangential velocity describes the motion of an object along the edge of the circle. Its direction at any given point on the circle is always along the tangent to that point. It is the linear component of angular velocity along the circular path. The SI unit of tangential velocity is meters per second (\(m/s\)).

Tangential velocity is equal to the tangential distance divided by time:

\(v_t = \frac{s}{t}\)

For one complete rotation, the tangential displacement \(s\) is the circumference \(2\pi r\), and the time taken is the period \(T\). So:

\(v_t = \frac{2\pi r}{T}\)

Relationship between Angular and Tangential Velocity

We know \(s = r\theta\) and \(v_t = s/t\).
Substituting \(s = r\theta\) into the tangential velocity equation:

\(v_t = \frac{r\theta}{t}\)

Since \(\omega = \theta/t\), we get:

\(v_t = r\omega\)

This equation shows the direct relationship between tangential velocity (\(v_t\)), radius (\(r\)), and angular velocity (\(\omega\)). Note that angular velocity is the same for all points on a rotating object, but tangential velocity depends directly on the distance from the axis of rotation (\(r\)).

Using the relationship \(v_t = r\omega\):

\(v_t = (0.3\,m)(40\,rad/s)\)

\(v_t = 12\,m/s\)

The tangential velocity of the wheel is \(12\,m/s\).

\(v_t \approx 3.14\,m/s\)

The tangential velocity of the point is approximately \(3.14\,m/s\).

c. Centripetal Acceleration: Acceleration in Uniform Circular Motion

We know that acceleration is caused by a change in velocity (magnitude, direction, or both). In uniform circular motion, the object's speed is constant, but its direction of motion continuously changes. This continuous change in direction means the object is always accelerating. This acceleration is called centripetal acceleration (\(a_c\)).

Centripetal means "center-seeking." Centripetal acceleration is always directed towards the center of the circular path. Because it points along the radius towards the center, it is also known as radial acceleration. The SI unit of centripetal acceleration is \(m/s^2\).

Key Term

Centripetal acceleration is an acceleration in circular motion caused by the change in the direction of velocity. Its direction is always towards the center of the circle.

\(a_c = \frac{(r\omega)^2}{r} = \frac{r^2\omega^2}{r}\)

Both formulas give the magnitude of centripetal acceleration, which is always directed towards the center of the circle.

  • \(v_2 = 90\,km/h = 90 \times \frac{1000}{3600} = \frac{900}{36} = 25\,m/s\)

Initial centripetal acceleration (\(a_{c1}\)):

\(a_{c1} = \frac{v_1^2}{r} = \frac{(25/3)^2}{r} = \frac{625/9}{r} \approx \frac{69.44}{r}\)

Final centripetal acceleration (\(a_{c2}\)):

\(a_{c2} = \frac{v_2^2}{r} = \frac{(25)^2}{r} = \frac{625}{r}\)

To find how much it changes, we can look at the ratio:

\(\frac{a_{c2}}{a_{c1}} = \frac{625/r}{625/9r} = \frac{625}{r} \times \frac{9r}{625} = 9\)

The centripetal acceleration increases by a factor of 9 when the speed triples from 30 km/h to 90 km/h. This demonstrates that centripetal acceleration is proportional to the square of the velocity, so even a small increase in speed can lead to a large increase in the required centripetal force.

d. Centripetal Force

For an object to move in a circular path, a force must constantly act on it, directed towards the center of the circle. This force is called centripetal force (\(F_c\)). Without it, an object would simply continue to move in a straight line, according to Newton's First Law of Motion. The centripetal force is always perpendicular to the tangential velocity vector.

From Newton's Second Law of Motion (\(F=ma\)), we can express centripetal force as:

\(F_c = ma_c\)

Substituting the expressions for centripetal acceleration (\(a_c = \frac{v^2}{r}\) or \(a_c = \omega^2 r\)):

\(F_c = m\frac{v^2}{r}\)

or

\(F_c = m\omega^2 r\)

The source of the centripetal force can vary: tension in a string, friction between tires and the road, gravitational force, or normal force components.

Key Term

Centripetal force is the force that causes centripetal acceleration. It is always perpendicular to the direction of motion of the object and points towards the center of the circular path.

ii. Motion of a Car Around a Roundabout or Banked Road

a. Car on a Roundabout (Flat Curve):
When a car moves around a roundabout on a flat surface, the frictional force between the road and the tires provides the necessary centripetal force. Without sufficient friction, the car would skid outwards.

\(F_{friction} = F_c = m\frac{v^2}{r}\)

The maximum static friction force is given by \(F_{friction,max} = \mu_s N\), where \(\mu_s\) is the coefficient of static friction and \(N\) is the normal force. For a flat road, \(N = mg\).

So, the condition for not skidding is \(\mu_s mg \geq m\frac{v^2}{r}\), which simplifies to:

\(v_{max} = \sqrt{\mu_s g r}\)

b. Motion Round a Banked Road:
On a banked road, the road surface is angled, which means a component of the normal force helps provide the centripetal force. This allows vehicles to navigate curves safely at higher speeds without relying solely on friction, especially in adverse conditions.

Consider a car moving around a banked road with radius \(r\) and banking angle \(\theta\). If there is no side-slip (friction is neglected), the forces are the normal force (\(N\)) and gravity (\(mg\)).

  • The horizontal component of the normal force provides the centripetal force: \(N \sin\theta = m\frac{v^2}{r}\)
  • The vertical component of the normal force balances gravity: \(N \cos\theta = mg\)

Dividing the first equation by the second:

\(\frac{N \sin\theta}{N \cos\theta} = \frac{m\frac{v^2}{r}}{mg}\)

\(\tan\theta = \frac{v^2}{rg}\)

This equation gives the ideal banking angle for a given speed and radius, where no friction is needed. The angle of banking can also be expressed as:

\(\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{v^2}{rg}\right)\)

iii. The Conical Pendulum

A conical pendulum consists of a mass \(m\) (a bob) fixed to the end of a string of length \(L\) and whirled around in a horizontal circle of radius \(r\) at a constant speed \(v\). The string traces out the surface of a cone, hence the name.

Let the angle the string makes with the vertical be \(\theta\). There are two forces acting on the bob:

  • The tension (\(T\)) in the string, acting along the string.
  • The weight (\(mg\)) of the bob, acting vertically downwards.

We can resolve the tension \(T\) into horizontal and vertical components:

  • Vertical component: \(T \cos\theta\) balances the weight of the bob.
    \(T \cos\theta = mg\)
  • Horizontal component: \(T \sin\theta\) acts towards the center of the circle and provides the centripetal force.
    \(T \sin\theta = \frac{mv^2}{r}\)

By dividing the horizontal equation by the vertical equation, we can eliminate the tension \(T\) and mass \(m\):

\(\frac{T \sin\theta}{T \cos\theta} = \frac{\frac{mv^2}{r}}{mg}\)

\(\tan\theta = \frac{v^2}{rg}\)

This result is identical to the equation for the banking angle of a road, showing that the physics governing the forces in a conical pendulum is the same as that for a car on a banked curve.

\(\sin\theta = \frac{0.5}{1.0} = 0.5\)

3.4 Two-dimensional Motion

3.4.1 Introduction to Two-dimensional Kinematics

Kinematics is the study of motion without considering its causes—that is, we analyze how things move without worrying about the forces pushing or pulling them. While linear motion deals with objects moving in a straight line, two-dimensional motion covers objects moving along curved paths.

Imagine a ball kicked by a football player, a bicycle rounding a curve, or the Moon orbiting Earth. These are all examples of motion in two dimensions. In fact, most motions in nature follow curved paths rather than straight lines.

To make analyzing this motion simple, we treat two-dimensional motion as two independent one-dimensional motions happening at the same time: one along the horizontal axis (x-axis) and one along the vertical axis (y-axis). These two components are completely independent of each other and are connected only by the variable of time, \( t \).

3.4.2 Projectile Motion

A projectile is any object that is thrown, fired, or released and moves mainly under the influence of gravity. Examples include a cannonball, a bullet, a golf ball, or even a stream of water from a hose. The path a projectile follows through the air is called its trajectory, which is shaped like a curve known as a parabola.

Assumptions for Ideal Projectile Motion

To analyze projectile motion effectively, we make three key assumptions:

  1. The free-fall acceleration due to gravity (\( g \)) is constant over the range of motion and is always directed downward (\( g \approx 9.8 \, m/s^2 \)).
  2. The effect of air resistance is negligible.
  3. The horizontal velocity is constant because gravity only acts vertically.

Horizontal Projection

In horizontal projection, an object is launched sideways from a height with an initial horizontal velocity, but zero initial vertical velocity. As it moves forward, gravity pulls it down, causing it to gain vertical speed.

Horizontal Component (Constant Velocity):
Since there is no acceleration in the x-direction, the velocity remains constant. \[ v_x = v_{0x} \] The horizontal distance traveled is: \[ \Delta x = v_{0x} t \]

Vertical Component (Constant Acceleration):
Gravity acts downwards. Since the object starts with no vertical push (\( v_{0y} = 0 \)), the velocity and displacement equations are: \[ v_y = gt \] \[ \Delta y = \frac{1}{2} g t^2 \]

Note: When calculating vertical motion, downwards motion is often treated as negative, and upwards as positive. However, for simple drops, we often focus on the magnitude.

Example 1: The Rifle Shot

A rifle is aimed horizontally at a target 30 m away. The bullet hits the target 2 cm below the aiming point. Assume \( g = 10 \, m/s^2 \).

(a) What is the bullet’s time of flight?

We use the vertical displacement equation. Note that 2 cm = 0.02 m. Since it falls down, displacement is negative.
\[ \Delta y = \frac{1}{2}gt^2 \] \[ -0.02 \, m = \frac{1}{2}(-10)t^2 \] \[ t^2 = \frac{0.04}{10} = 0.004 \] \[ t = 0.06 \, s \]

(b) What is the initial velocity of the bullet?

The horizontal speed is constant.
\[ v_{0x} = \frac{\Delta x}{t} \] \[ v_{0x} = \frac{30 \, m}{0.06 \, s} = 500 \, m/s \]

Example 2: The Rescue Plane

A rescue airplane travelling at 360 km/h horizontally drops a food package from a height of 300 m. Assume \( g = 10 \, m/s^2 \).

(a) How long will it take the food package to reach the ground?

Initial vertical velocity is zero. \[ \Delta y = \frac{1}{2}gt^2 \] \[ -300 = \frac{1}{2}(-10)t^2 \] \[ t^2 = 60 \Rightarrow t = 7.74 \, s \]

(b) How far from the car driver should the food package be dropped?

First, convert speed to m/s: \( 360 \, km/h = 100 \, m/s \). \[ \Delta x = v_{0x}t \] \[ \Delta x = 100 \, m/s \times 7.74 \, s = 774 \, m \]

3.4.3 Inclined Projectile Motion

When an object is launched at an angle \( \theta \) with the horizontal, it has both initial horizontal and vertical velocities. We must resolve the initial velocity \( v_0 \) into components:

  • Horizontal initial velocity: \( v_{0x} = v_0 \cos \theta \)
  • Vertical initial velocity: \( v_{0y} = v_0 \sin \theta \)

The horizontal velocity remains constant throughout the flight (assuming no air resistance), while the vertical velocity changes due to gravity.

Key Equations for Inclined Projection

Horizontal position at time \( t \):
\( \Delta x = (v_0 \cos \theta)t \)

Vertical position at time \( t \):
\( \Delta y = (v_0 \sin \theta)t + \frac{1}{2}gt^2 \)

Vertical velocity at time \( t \):
\( v_y = v_0 \sin \theta + gt \)

Special Parameters of Projectile Motion

1. Time to Maximum Height: At the peak of the flight, the vertical velocity \( v_y \) is momentarily zero. \[ t_{peak} = \frac{v_0 \sin \theta}{g} \] (Note: here we use the magnitude of g, usually 9.8 or 10).

2. Total Time of Flight: If the projectile lands at the same height it launched from, the total time is twice the time to the peak. \[ T_{total} = \frac{2v_0 \sin \theta}{g} \]

3. Maximum Height (H): \[ H = \frac{v_0^2 \sin^2 \theta}{2g} \]

4. Horizontal Range (R): The maximum horizontal distance covered. \[ R = \frac{v_0^2 \sin 2\theta}{g} \] From this equation, we can see that the range is maximum when the launch angle is \( 45^\circ \) (since \( \sin(90^\circ) = 1 \)). Also, two angles that add up to \( 90^\circ \) (complementary angles) will result in the same range.

Example 1: Football Kick

A football player kicks a ball at an angle of \( 37^\circ \) with the horizontal. The initial velocity of the ball is 40 m/s. (Use \( g = 10 \, m/s^2 \)).

(a) Find the maximum height.

\[ H = \frac{v_0^2 \sin^2 \theta}{2g} = \frac{(40)^2 (\sin 37^\circ)^2}{2 \times 10} \] \[ H = \frac{1600 \times (0.6)^2}{20} = \frac{1600 \times 0.36}{20} = 28.8 \, m \]

(b) Find the horizontal range.

\[ R = \frac{v_0^2 \sin 2\theta}{g} = \frac{(40)^2 \sin(74^\circ)}{10} \] \[ R = \frac{1600 \times 0.96}{10} = 153.6 \, m \] (approx 153.8 m in text)

Example 2: Ball Hitting a Wall

A ball is kicked from the ground with an initial speed of 25 m/s at an angle of \( 53^\circ \) towards a wall located 24 m away.

How high above the ground does the ball hit the wall?

First, find the time it takes to travel horizontal distance \( \Delta x = 24 \, m \). \[ \Delta x = v_0 \cos \theta \, t \] \[ 24 = 25 (\cos 53^\circ) t \] \[ t = \frac{24}{25 \times 0.6} = \frac{24}{15} = 1.6 \, s \]

Now, find the vertical height \( \Delta y \) at \( t = 1.6 \, s \). \[ \Delta y = (v_0 \sin \theta)t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2 \] \[ \Delta y = (25 \times 0.8)(1.6) - 0.5(10)(1.6)^2 \] \[ \Delta y = (20)(1.6) - 5(2.56) \] \[ \Delta y = 32 - 12.8 = 19.2 \, m \] The ball hits the wall 19.2 m above the ground.

3.4.4 Rotational Motion

Rotational motion is the movement of an object in a circle around a fixed axis. Think of the rotation of Earth, a spinning CD, or the wheels of a moving car. A rigid body is an object with a perfectly defined shape that does not deform; the distance between any two particles inside it remains constant.

Angular Quantities

  • Angular Displacement (\( \Delta \theta \)): The angle through which an object rotates. It is measured in radians (rad). (Recall: \( 1 \, \text{revolution} = 2\pi \, \text{rad} = 360^\circ \)).
  • Angular Velocity (\( \omega \)): How fast an object rotates. \[ \omega_{av} = \frac{\Delta \theta}{\Delta t} \] Unit: radians per second (rad/s).
  • Angular Acceleration (\( \alpha \)): The rate at which angular velocity changes. \[ \alpha = \frac{\Delta \omega}{\Delta t} \] Unit: radians per second squared (rad/s²).

Direction: Angular velocity and acceleration are vectors. We determine their direction using the Right-Hand Rule: Curl the fingers of your right hand in the direction of rotation. Your thumb points in the direction of the angular velocity vector.

Kinematic Equations for Constant Angular Acceleration

The equations for rotational motion are analogous to linear motion.

Linear Motion Rotational Motion
\( v_f = v_0 + at \) \( \omega_f = \omega_0 + \alpha t \)
\( \Delta s = \frac{v_f + v_0}{2} t \) \( \Delta \theta = \frac{\omega_f + \omega_0}{2} t \)
\( \Delta s = v_0 t + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \) \( \Delta \theta = \omega_0 t + \frac{1}{2}\alpha t^2 \)
\( v_f^2 = v_0^2 + 2a \Delta s \) \( \omega_f^2 = \omega_0^2 + 2\alpha \Delta \theta \)

Relationship between Linear and Angular Quantities

When a rigid body rotates, points on the body move in circles. Points further from the center move faster linearly, even though they have the same angular speed.

  • Arc length (Distance): \( s = r \theta \)
  • Tangential Velocity: \( v = r \omega \)
  • Tangential Acceleration: \( a_t = r \alpha \)

Example 1: Accelerating Wheel

A rotating wheel has an initial angular velocity of 10 rad/s and accelerates at \( 2.5 \, rad/s^2 \). How many revolutions are completed in 30 s?

Use the kinematic equation: \[ \Delta \theta = \omega_0 t + \frac{1}{2}\alpha t^2 \] \[ \Delta \theta = (10)(30) + \frac{1}{2}(2.5)(30^2) \] \[ \Delta \theta = 300 + 1.25(900) \] \[ \Delta \theta = 300 + 1125 = 1425 \, rad \]

Convert to revolutions: \[ \text{Revolutions} = \frac{1425}{2\pi} \approx 226.9 \, \text{rev} \]

Example 2: Bicycle Speed

The angular velocity of a bicycle wheel is 18 rad/s. If the radius of the wheel is 40 cm, what is the speed of the bicycle?

Convert radius to meters: \( r = 0.4 \, m \).
The linear speed of the bike equals the tangential speed of the wheel's rim. \[ v = \omega r \] \[ v = 18 \, rad/s \times 0.4 \, m = 7.2 \, m/s \]

3.4.5 Rotational Dynamics

Just as force causes linear acceleration, Torque causes angular acceleration.

Torque (\( \tau \)): The rotational equivalent of force. It depends on the magnitude of the force applied, the distance from the pivot (radius), and the angle of application. \[ \tau = r F \sin \theta \] SI Unit: Newton-meter (Nm).

Moment of Inertia (\( I \)): The rotational equivalent of mass. It measures an object's resistance to rotational motion. For a single particle: \[ I = m r^2 \] For a system of particles, \( I = \sum m r^2 \). The unit is \( kg \cdot m^2 \).

Newton's Second Law for Rotation: Just as \( F = ma \), for rotation we have: \[ \tau_{net} = I \alpha \]

Example 1: Calculating Torque

A force \( F_1 = 10 \, N \) acts on a bar at a distance \( r_1 = 3.0 \, m \) from the pivot. The angle is \( 120^\circ \). What is the torque?

\[ \tau = r F \sin \theta \] \[ \tau = 3.0 \times 10 \times \sin(120^\circ) \] \[ \tau = 30 \times 0.866 \approx 25.98 \, Nm \]

Example 2: Angular Acceleration from Torque

A motor produces a constant torque of 100 Nm attached to a flywheel with rotational inertia \( 0.1 \, kg \cdot m^2 \). What is the angular acceleration?

Using Newton's Second Law for Rotation: \[ \tau = I \alpha \] \[ \alpha = \frac{\tau}{I} = \frac{100}{0.1} = 1000 \, rad/s^2 \]

3.4.6 Planetary Motion and Kepler’s Laws

Johannes Kepler analyzed astronomical data to formulate three laws that describe how planets orbit the Sun.

Kepler's Three Laws

  1. The Law of Ellipses: Planets orbit the Sun in an ellipse, with the Sun located at one of the two foci.
  2. The Law of Equal Areas: A line drawn from the Sun to a planet sweeps out equal areas in equal time intervals. This means planets move faster when closer to the Sun (perihelion) and slower when further away (aphelion).
  3. The Law of Harmonics (Third Law): The square of the orbital period (\( T \)) is proportional to the cube of the average distance (\( R \)) from the Sun. \[ \frac{T^2}{R^3} = K \] Where \( K \) is a constant for all planets in the solar system (\( \approx 2.97 \times 10^{-19} s^2/m^3 \)).

Example 1: Pluto's Period

Earth's period \( T_E = 365 \) days and distance \( R_E = 1.495 \times 10^8 \) km. Pluto's distance \( R_P = 5.896 \times 10^9 \) km. Find Pluto's period.

Using Kepler's 3rd Law ratio: \[ \frac{T_E^2}{R_E^3} = \frac{T_P^2}{R_P^3} \] \[ T_P^2 = T_E^2 \left( \frac{R_P}{R_E} \right)^3 \] \[ T_P = T_E \sqrt{ \left( \frac{5.896 \times 10^9}{1.495 \times 10^8} \right)^3 } \] \[ T_P \approx 365 \times \sqrt{(39.4)^3} \approx 9.0 \times 10^{14} \, \text{days} \]

(note: calculation simplified)

Example 2: Saturn's Distance

Saturn is on average 9 times farther from the Sun than Earth (\( R_S = 9R_E \)). What is its period in Earth years?

\[ \frac{T_S^2}{R_S^3} = \frac{T_E^2}{R_E^3} \] \[ T_S^2 = (1 \, \text{year})^2 \left( \frac{9R_E}{R_E} \right)^3 \] \[ T_S^2 = 1 \times 9^3 = 729 \] \[ T_S = \sqrt{729} = 27 \, \text{years} \]

3.4.7 Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation

Isaac Newton realized that the force causing apples to fall is the same force keeping the Moon in orbit. He formulated the Law of Universal Gravitation.

Every particle of matter attracts every other particle with a force directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. \[ F_g = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2} \] Where \( G \) is the universal gravitational constant, \( G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \, Nm^2/kg^2 \).

Relationship to Gravity on Earth

We can derive the acceleration due to gravity (\( g \)) on Earth's surface using this law. If \( M_E \) is Earth's mass and \( r_E \) is Earth's radius: \[ mg = G \frac{M_E m}{r_E^2} \Rightarrow g = G \frac{M_E}{r_E^2} \]

Deriving Kepler's Third Law

Gravity provides the centripetal force needed for planetary orbit. \[ F_c = F_g \] \[ \frac{m_p v^2}{r} = G \frac{M_s m_p}{r^2} \] Substituting \( v = \frac{2\pi r}{T} \), we arrive at Kepler's formula: \( \frac{T^2}{r^3} = \frac{4\pi^2}{GM_s} \).

Example 1: Gravitational Force

A 10 kg mass and a 100 kg mass are 1 meter apart. Find the force of attraction.

\[ F_g = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2} \] \[ F_g = (6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \frac{10 \times 100}{1^2} \] \[ F_g = 6.67 \times 10^{-8} \, N \]

Example 2: Weight Calculation

Calculate the gravitational attraction on a 60 kg person on Earth's surface. (\( M_E = 5.97 \times 10^{24} \, kg, r_E = 6.38 \times 10^6 \, m \)).

\[ F = G \frac{M_E m}{r^2} \] \[ F = (6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \frac{(5.97 \times 10^{24})(60)}{(6.38 \times 10^6)^2} \] \[ F \approx 584 \, N \] (Checking via \( F=mg \): \( 60 \times 9.8 = 588 \, N \). The values are very close).

Summary of Key Concepts

  • Motion in a straight line is one of the simplest forms of motion in a specific direction and is called rectilinear motion.
  • An object is in motion if it changes position relative to a reference point.
  • Motion can be described by distance, speed, displacement, and velocity, where displacement and velocity also include direction.
  • Distance is a physical quantity which describes the length between two points (places) and is the total path length travelled by a body. Distance is a scalar quantity.
  • Displacement is the change in position of a body in a certain direction.
  • The speed of an object can be calculated by dividing the distance traveled by the time needed to travel the distance.
  • The velocity of an object is the speed of the object with its direction of motion.
  • Average velocity is displacement over the time period during which the displacement occurs. If the velocity is constant, then average velocity and instantaneous velocity are the same.
  • Acceleration occurs whenever an object speeds up, slows down, or changes direction.
  • Uniform motion is the motion of an object along a straight line with a constant velocity or speed in a given direction.
  • The slope of a position-versus-time graph at a specific time gives instantaneous velocity at that time.
  • On a speed-time graph, a horizontal line represents zero acceleration or constant speed.
  • An object is in motion if it changes position relative to a reference point.
  • Distance is the total path length, a scalar quantity.
  • Displacement is the change in position (final minus initial), a vector quantity.
  • Average velocity is displacement divided by time: \( v_{av} = \frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t} \).
  • Instantaneous velocity is the velocity at a specific moment in time.
  • Average acceleration is the change in velocity divided by time: \( a_{av} = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} \).
  • Instantaneous acceleration is the acceleration at a specific moment in time.
  • For uniformly accelerated motion, the key equations are:
    • \( v = v_0 + at \)
    • \( s = v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \)
    • \( v^2 = v_0^2 + 2as \)
  • In free fall, acceleration \(a\) is replaced by \(g\) (acceleration due to gravity), and displacement \(s\) by height \(h\).
  • A position-time graph for uniformly accelerated motion is a parabola; its tangent's slope is instantaneous velocity.
  • A velocity-time graph for uniformly accelerated motion is a straight line; its slope is acceleration, and its area is displacement.
  • An acceleration-time graph for uniformly accelerated motion is a horizontal line; its area is the change in velocity.
  • Relative velocity is the velocity of one object with respect to another observer or object.
    • If objects move in the same direction, relative velocity is the difference in magnitudes.
    • If objects move in opposite directions, relative velocity is the sum of their magnitudes.
  • Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity per unit time. It is a vector quantity measured in \(m/s^2\).
    • Average acceleration: \(a_{av} = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}\)
    • Instantaneous acceleration: \(a = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}\)
  • Uniformly Accelerated Motion Equations (for constant acceleration \(a\)):
    • \(v_f = v_i + at\)
    • \(s = v_it + \frac{1}{2}at^2\)
    • \(v_f^2 = v_i^2 + 2as\)
    • \(s = \left(\frac{v_i + v_f}{2}\right)t\)
  • Stopping Distance is the sum of reaction distance and braking distance: \(s_{total} = vt + \frac{v^2}{2\mu g}\).
  • Graphical Representation:
    • Slope of displacement-time graph = Velocity.
    • Slope of velocity-time graph = Acceleration.
    • Area under velocity-time graph = Displacement.
  • Free Fall is vertical motion under gravity alone. The acceleration is \(g \approx 9.8\,m/s^2\) downwards. Equations are modified by replacing \(a\) with \(-g\) (if up is positive).
  • Uniform Circular Motion is motion in a circle at constant speed but changing direction (continuously accelerating).
    • Angular Velocity (\(\omega\)): Rate of change of angular displacement (\(\omega = \Delta\theta / \Delta t\)).
    • Relationship: Tangential velocity \(v_t = r\omega\).
    • Centripetal Acceleration (\(a_c\)): Directed towards the center. \(a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = \omega^2 r\).
    • Centripetal Force (\(F_c\)): The net force causing circular motion. \(F_c = ma_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}\).
  • Projectile Motion: Motion in 2D under gravity. Horizontal velocity is constant; vertical motion has constant acceleration (\( g \)).
  • Independence of Motion: The time \( t \) to cover horizontal and vertical distances is the same.
  • Max Range: Achieved at a \( 45^\circ \) launch angle.
  • Rotational Variables: Angular displacement (\( \theta \)), velocity (\( \omega \)), and acceleration (\( \alpha \)) are the rotational analogs of \( s, v, a \).
  • Connection Formula: Linear quantities relate to angular ones by the radius: \( s=r\theta, v=r\omega, a_t=r\alpha \).
  • Torque (\( \tau \)): The cause of angular acceleration (\( \tau = I\alpha \)).
  • Kepler's Laws: Describe planetary orbits (Ellipses, Equal Areas, Period-Distance relationship).
  • Universal Gravitation: Gravity acts between all masses: \( F_g \propto \frac{1}{r^2} \).
