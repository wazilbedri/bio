Physics begins with observations of phenomena, events, matter, or energy. Through demanding and controlled experimentation and logical thought processes, physical phenomena are described quantitatively using mathematical tools. Any quantitative description of a property requires comparison with a scale of different measuring devices.

For example, to measure length, you need a meter stick; for time, a watch; and for mass, a beam balance. In this process, we recognize that different kinds of properties cannot be compared directly. You cannot compare the time it takes to travel from point A to point B with the distance between these two points, even though the two quantities might be related.

The time of travel (time) is a physical quantity, and the distance (length) is also a physical quantity. They are completely different types of physical quantities, measured by different devices and units.

2.1 Physical Quantities

2.1.1 Scales, Standards, Units (Prefixes)

i. Scales

A scale on a measuring device contains markings that show a certain amount of whatever is being measured. The number of marks on a device determines how accurate a measurement can be. More marks mean greater precision. For instance, if a scale has numbered values with a difference of 1 between them, and ten spaces in between, each space represents one-tenth (0.1) of the distance to the next larger number. Measurements with such a device can be precise to two decimal places, allowing for an estimated last digit.

The red line goes just past 3.3 but not quite to 3.4. We can estimate the second decimal place. It looks like the line goes roughly halfway between 3.3 and 3.4. So, we will say 3.35.

If you need to report measurement results so that someone else can reproduce them, a standard must be defined. A chosen standard must:

  • Be easily accessible.
  • Possess a property that can be measured reliably.
  • Yield the same result regardless of who takes the measurement or where it's taken.

In 2019, an International Committee revised a set of standards for length, mass, time, and other basic quantities. This system, an adaptation of the metric system, is called the SI system of units.

Length

The meter (m) is the standard (SI) unit for length. Other common non-SI units include centimeter (cm), millimeter (mm), and kilometer (km). Today, the meter (m) is defined as the distance traveled by light in vacuum during a time of \( \frac{1}{299792458} \) seconds.

Time

Time is defined as the interval between two events. It is a fundamental quantity. The SI unit of time is the second (s). Non-SI units include minute (min), hour (hr), day, month, and year. The second (s) is defined as 9,192,631,770 times the period of vibration of radiation from the caesium-133 atom.

Mass

The kilogram (kg) is the standard (SI) unit of mass. Non-SI units include gram (g), milligram (mg), and tonne. The kilogram (kg) is defined by taking the fixed numerical value of the Planck constant \(h = 6.62607015 \times 10^{-34}\) when expressed in units of J s (which equals \( \text{kg m}^2/\text{s} \)), where the meter and second are defined in terms of the speed of light in vacuum (\(c\)) and the frequency of the Caesium 133 atom (\( \Delta f \)). Specifically, \(1 \text{ kg} = 1.4755214 \times 10^{40} \frac{h \Delta f}{c^2}\).

iii. Scientific Notation

In physics, scientific notation is a way to write measured values that are too large or too small to be conveniently written as a decimal. It follows the general form:

\( d \times 10^n \)

Here, \(d\) is a decimal number between 0 and 10, rounded off to a few decimal places. \(n\) is the exponent, an integer. If \(n > 0\), the decimal place in \(d\) should be moved to the right \(n\) times. If \(n < 0\), it should be moved to the left \(n\) times.

For example:

  • \(3.24 \times 10^3\) represents 3240 (decimal moved three places to the right).
  • \(3.24 \times 10^{-3}\) represents 0.00324 (decimal moved three places to the left).

Example 1: Converting to Scientific Notation

Write 0.000001256 in scientific notation to 3 decimal places.

To convert 0.000001256 to scientific notation, we move the decimal point to the right until there is only one non-zero digit before it. We move it 6 places to the right to get 1.256. Since we moved it to the right, the exponent will be negative.

So, 0.000001256 becomes \(1.256 \times 10^{-6}\).

Example 2: Identifying Significant Figures

How many significant figures are in 7800?

In a number like 7800, the trailing zeros are not considered significant unless they are explicitly indicated to be (e.g., by a decimal point). Therefore, only the non-zero digits are significant.

The number 7800 has two significant figures (7 and 8).

iv. Significant Figures

In a number, every non-zero digit is a significant figure. Zeros are only counted as significant if they are between two non-zero digits or at the end of the decimal part.

  • For example, the number 2000 has 1 significant figure (the 2).
  • The number 2000.0 has 5 significant figures.

You can estimate a number by removing significant figures (starting from the right) and rounding as you go. For example, 6.827 has 4 significant figures. If you want to write it to 3 significant figures, you remove the 7 and round up, making it 6.83.

When adding or subtracting experimentally measured values with different significant figures (digits), follow this rule:

  • The number of significant figures or digits in the result should be equal to the smallest number of decimal places of any term in the sum or difference.
  • Example: When adding 9.65 and 8.4, the term with the fewest decimal places is 8.4 (one decimal place). So, the sum is 18.1, not 18.05.

When multiplying or dividing experimentally measured values with different significant figures (digits), follow this rule:

  • The number of significant digits in the final answer should be the same as the quantity with the smallest number of significant figures.
  • Example: When multiplying 8.65 (3 significant figures) and 2.035 (4 significant figures), the answer should have 3 significant figures. So, \(8.65 \times 2.035 = 17.75845\), which rounds to 17.8.

Example 1: Significant Figures in Addition

Write the number for the expression \(1.513 + 27.3\) with an appropriate number of significant figures.

The numbers are 1.513 (three decimal places) and 27.3 (one decimal place). The result should have the same number of decimal places as the term with the fewest decimal places, which is one.

\(1.513 + 27.3 = 28.813\)

Rounding to one decimal place, the answer is 28.8.

Example 2: Significant Figures in Multiplication

Write the number for the expression \(2.1 \times 5.687\) with an appropriate number of significant figures.

The numbers are 2.1 (two significant figures) and 5.687 (four significant figures). The result should have the same number of significant figures as the term with the fewest, which is two.

\(2.1 \times 5.687 = 11.9427\)

Rounding to two significant figures, the answer is 12.

  • The physical quantity to be measured.
  • The necessary measuring tools.
  • Units of measurements used (standard units).

Measurement is the comparison of an unknown quantity with a known fixed unit quantity. It has two parts: the unit and the number (magnitude) indicating how many units are in the quantity being measured.

2.1.3 Classification of Physical Quantities

Some physical quantities are measured directly, while others are measured by combining two or more measurable quantities. For instance, you might measure the length and width of a blackboard directly. However, the area is calculated by multiplying length and width (\(A = l \times w\)). Physical quantities can be classified into two main types:

  • Fundamental or basic physical quantities.
  • Derived physical quantities.

i. Fundamental or Basic Physical Quantities

Fundamental or basic physical quantities are quantities that can be measured directly. They cannot be described in terms of other physical quantities. The units used to measure fundamental quantities are called fundamental units. A fundamental unit does not depend on any other unit. There are seven fundamental physical quantities:

Mass \(m\) kilogram kg
Time \(t\) Second s
Temperature \(T\) Kelvin K
Current \(I\) Ampere A

Luminous intensity \(I_v\) Candela cd

ii. Derived Physical Quantities

Derived physical quantities are quantities that depend on one or more fundamental quantities for their measurements. The units of derived quantities, which depend on fundamental units, are called derived units.

Examples of derived physical quantities include area, volume, density, and speed. Here's a table showing some derived quantities and their units:

Acceleration \(a\) \(\frac{\text{Velocity}}{\text{Time}}\) meter per second square \(\frac{\text{m}}{\text{s}^2}\)

Example 1: Describing Derived Quantities

Describe volume, density, and speed as combinations of fundamental physical quantities.

  • Volume: Volume is derived from length. For a cube, Volume = length \(\times\) width \(\times\) height. Since width and height are also forms of length, Volume = length \(\times\) length \(\times\) length = \(\text{length}^3\).
  • Density: Density is defined as mass per unit volume. So, Density = \(\frac{\text{Mass}}{\text{Volume}}\). This combines the fundamental quantity of mass with the derived quantity of volume (which is based on length).
  • Speed: Speed is defined as distance traveled per unit time. So, Speed = \(\frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Time}}\). This combines the fundamental quantities of length (distance) and time.

Example 2: Units of Derived Quantities

Determine the units of volume, density, and speed using basic units.

  • Unit of Volume: Since Volume = \(\text{length}^3\), and the basic unit of length is the meter (m), the unit of volume is \(\text{meter}^3\) or \(\text{m}^3\).
  • Unit of Density: Since Density = \(\frac{\text{Mass}}{\text{Volume}}\), and the basic unit of mass is the kilogram (kg) and the unit of volume is \(\text{m}^3\), the unit of density is \(\frac{\text{kilogram}}{\text{meter}^3}\) or \(\text{kg/m}^3\).
  • Unit of Speed: Since Speed = \(\frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Time}}\), and the basic unit of distance (length) is the meter (m) and the basic unit of time is the second (s), the unit of speed is \(\frac{\text{meter}}{\text{second}}\) or \(\text{m/s}\).

iii. Scalar and Vector Quantities

Physical quantities can also be classified as scalar or vector quantities. Some physical quantities are described completely by a number and a unit. A number with a unit is called a magnitude. However, other quantities also have a direction attached to the magnitude. These are grouped into two:

  • Scalar quantities
  • Vector quantities

A scalar quantity is a physical quantity that has only magnitude but no direction. Examples include distance, mass, time, temperature, and energy.

A vector quantity is a physical quantity that has both magnitude and direction. For instance, when we say a car moves 50 km/h to the east, we are giving full information about its velocity: 50 km/h is the magnitude, and east is the direction. Because it includes direction, velocity is a vector quantity.

Examples of vector quantities are displacement, acceleration, and force.

A vector can be represented by a single letter in bold face (e.g., \(\mathbf{S}\)) or by a single letter with an arrow above it (e.g., \(\vec{S}\)).

Example 1: Classifying Physical Quantities

Classify the following physical quantities as scalar or vector quantity: mass, time, area, speed, velocity, acceleration, force, energy, work, pressure, momentum, electric current, current density, displacement, and temperature.

  • Scalar Quantities (magnitude only): mass, time, area, speed, energy, work, pressure, electric current, temperature.
  • Vector Quantities (magnitude and direction): velocity, acceleration, force, momentum, current density, displacement.

Example 2: Understanding Magnitude and Direction

Explain why "walking 5 kilometers" describes a scalar quantity, but "walking 5 kilometers north" describes a vector quantity.

When you say "walking 5 kilometers," you are describing only the magnitude of the distance covered (5 kilometers). This is a scalar quantity because it doesn't specify the direction of travel. You could have walked 5 kilometers in any direction, or even in a circle.

However, when you say "walking 5 kilometers north," you are providing both the magnitude (5 kilometers) and the direction (north). This complete description makes it a vector quantity, specifically displacement. Displacement tells us not just how far you went, but also in what direction from your starting point.

2.2 Vector Quantities

In science, especially physics, making precise measurements is crucial. Accurate measurements have often led to new discoveries and important advancements throughout history. Any number or set of numbers used to describe a physical phenomenon quantitatively is called a physical quantity.

Physical quantities are generally categorized into two main groups: scalars and vectors. Scalars are quantities that only have magnitude (a numerical value), while vectors possess both magnitude and direction. Understanding scalars and vectors is fundamental to grasping various natural phenomena in physics.

2.2.1 Scalars and Vectors

Physical quantities can be completely described by a number and an appropriate unit. For example, saying an object's length is \(1.42 \text{ m}\) or its mass is \(12.21 \text{ kg}\) provides a full description. Similarly, the statement that water's density is \(1000 \text{ kg/m}^3\) fully describes its density. Quantities that can be specified in this straightforward way are called scalar quantities.

Scalar quantities are physical quantities that can be completely specified by a single number along with an appropriate unit of measurement. Examples include time, distance, speed, length, volume, temperature, energy, and power.

On the other hand, some quantities require both magnitude and direction for a complete description. For example, saying a train's velocity is \(100 \text{ km/h}\) isn't enough; you also need to know its direction. Similarly, with force, you must specify both the strength of the force and the direction in which it's applied. These quantities are called vectors.

A vector quantity has both magnitude and direction. Examples include displacement, velocity, acceleration, momentum, impulse, weight, and electric field strength.

Example 1: Identifying Scalars and Vectors

Classify the following physical quantities as either scalar or vector:

  1. \(25 \text{ kg}\) of apples
  2. A car moving at \(60 \text{ km/h}\) to the North
  3. A temperature of \(30^\circ \text{C}\)
  4. A force of \(100 \text{ N}\) pulling an object upwards

Solution:

  1. \(25 \text{ kg}\) of apples: This is a measure of mass, which only has magnitude. So, it's a scalar.
  2. A car moving at \(60 \text{ km/h}\) to the North: This includes both speed (\(60 \text{ km/h}\)) and direction (North). So, it's a vector (velocity).
  3. A temperature of \(30^\circ \text{C}\): This only has magnitude. So, it's a scalar.
  4. A force of \(100 \text{ N}\) pulling an object upwards: This includes both magnitude (\(100 \text{ N}\)) and direction (upwards). So, it's a vector.

Solution:

  • Distance: The total length of your walk to the nearest shop (e.g., \(500 \text{ meters}\)).

Vector Quantities:

  • Force: The push you apply to open a door (e.g., \(20 \text{ N}\) outwards).

2.2.2 Vector Representations

Vectors are unique because they have both magnitude and direction. They can be represented in two ways: algebraically or geometrically.

i. Algebraic Representation

Algebraically, a vector is typically represented by a bold letter, like \(\mathbf{A}\), or by a letter with an arrow placed over it, like \(\vec{A}\). For instance, a displacement can be described as \(\vec{S} = 50 \text{ km}\), Southwest. Here, \(\mathbf{S} = 50 \text{ km}\) specifically refers to the magnitude of the vector. The magnitude can also be shown by placing the absolute value notation around the symbol for the vector, so you might see it written as \(|\vec{S}|\).

ii. Geometrical Representation

Geometrically, vectors are drawn as an arrow or an arrow-tipped line segment. This arrow visually represents the vector. Its length is drawn to scale to show the magnitude of the vector, and the arrow's tip (the arrowhead) points in the specified direction. The starting point of the arrow is called the tail, and the ending point with the arrowhead is called the head. Thus, the length of the arrow indicates the vector's magnitude (when drawn to scale), and the arrowhead indicates the vector's direction.

Vectors are represented either algebraically (bold letter or arrow over letter) or geometrically (an arrow whose length represents magnitude and arrowhead shows direction).

To draw a vector accurately, you need to set a scale and include a reference direction in your diagram. You'll use a ruler to draw vectors to the correct length, ensuring the arrow's length is proportional to the quantity's magnitude. For example, if you set a scale where \(1 \text{ cm}\) represents \(2 \text{ N}\), then a force of \(20 \text{ N}\) to the East would be an arrow \(10 \text{ cm}\) long. Choosing a scale that results in a reasonably sized diagram is important. A reference direction could be a horizontal line or compass points.

The procedure for drawing vectors graphically is as follows:

  1. Decide on a suitable scale and write it down.
  2. Calculate the length of the arrow needed to represent the vector using your chosen scale.
  3. Draw the vector as an arrow, making sure to include the arrowhead.
  4. Label the vector with its magnitude.

Example 1: Drawing a Displacement Vector

Draw the vector representing a displacement of \(10 \text{ km}\) West, indicating the scale you used.

Solution:

  1. Decide on a scale: Let \(1 \text{ cm}\) represent \(2 \text{ km}\).
  2. Determine arrow length: For \(10 \text{ km}\), the arrow length will be \(10 \text{ km} / (2 \text{ km/cm}) = 5 \text{ cm}\).
  3. Draw the vector: Draw a \(5 \text{ cm}\) long arrow pointing West.
  4. Label: Label it " \(10 \text{ km}\) West".

Your drawing would show a \(5 \text{ cm}\) arrow pointing left, labeled " \(10 \text{ km}\)".

Example 2: Drawing a Force Vector

Represent a force of \(15 \text{ N}\) acting at an angle of \(45^\circ\) North of East graphically.

Solution:

  1. Decide on a scale: Let \(1 \text{ cm}\) represent \(5 \text{ N}\).
  2. Determine arrow length: For \(15 \text{ N}\), the arrow length will be \(15 \text{ N} / (5 \text{ N/cm}) = 3 \text{ cm}\).
  3. Draw the vector: Draw a coordinate system. From the origin, draw a \(3 \text{ cm}\) long arrow at a \(45^\circ\) angle from the East axis towards the North.
  4. Label: Label it " \(15 \text{ N}\)".

Your drawing would show an arrow \(3 \text{ cm}\) long pointing into the upper-right quadrant at \(45^\circ\) from the horizontal, labeled " \(15 \text{ N}\)".

There are several types of vectors:

  1. Zero vector or Null vector: A vector with zero magnitude and no specific direction.
  2. Unit Vector: A vector that has a magnitude equal to one. These are often used to indicate direction.
  3. Equal vectors: Two vectors are considered equal if they have both the same magnitude and the same direction.
  4. Negative of a vector: A vector that has the same magnitude as a given vector but points in the exact opposite direction.

Summary of Vector Representation:

  • Algebraically, a vector is shown by a bold letter or an arrow over the letter (e.g., \(\mathbf{A}\) or \(\vec{A}\)).
  • Geometrically, a vector is represented by an arrow. The length of this arrow indicates the vector's magnitude, and its arrowhead shows the vector's direction.

2.2.3 Vector Addition and Subtraction

Unlike scalar quantities, which are added through simple arithmetic (e.g., \(2 \text{ kg} + 3 \text{ kg} = 5 \text{ kg}\)), adding vectors is more complex because it involves both magnitude and direction. It's important to remember that only vectors of the same type can be added; for example, you can add two forces or two velocities, but not a force and a velocity.

  • Scalars and vectors cannot be added together.
  • For any two vectors to be added, they must represent the same physical quantity (e.g., force with force, displacement with displacement).

The result of adding a number of vectors is called the resultant vector. This single vector has the same overall effect as all the individual vectors acting together.

i. Addition of Vectors

If vectors \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\) are combined to produce a resultant vector \(\vec{R}\), this can be expressed mathematically as: \( \vec{R} = \vec{A} + \vec{B} \).

Example 1: Adding two parallel displacement vectors

You walk \(5 \text{ meters}\) East, then continue walking \(3 \text{ meters}\) further East. What is your total displacement?

Solution:

Since both displacements are in the same direction (East), they are parallel vectors. The resultant displacement is simply the sum of their magnitudes, and the direction remains East.

\(|\vec{R}| = |5 \text{ m}| + |3 \text{ m}| = 8 \text{ m}\)

So, your total displacement is \(8 \text{ meters}\) East.

Example 2: Adding two forces in the same direction

Two children push a toy car. One pushes with a force of \(20 \text{ N}\) to the right, and the other pushes with a force of \(15 \text{ N}\) also to the right. What is the total force on the toy car?

Solution:

Since both forces are in the same direction, we add their magnitudes.

\(|\vec{R}| = |20 \text{ N}| + |15 \text{ N}| = 35 \text{ N}\)

The total force on the toy car is \(35 \text{ N}\) to the right.

ii. Subtraction of Vectors

Vector subtraction is an extension of vector addition. To subtract vector \(\vec{B}\) from vector \(\vec{A}\) (written as \(\vec{A} - \vec{B}\)), you first need to understand the concept of a negative vector. The negative of vector \(\vec{B}\), denoted as \(-\vec{B}\), has the same magnitude as \(\vec{B}\) but points in the exact opposite direction. You essentially "flip" the vector's direction.

The subtraction of \(\vec{B}\) from \(\vec{A}\) is then defined as the addition of \(-\vec{B}\) to \(\vec{A}\):

\( \vec{A} - \vec{B} = \vec{A} + (-\vec{B}) \)

This means that vector subtraction is simply adding a negative vector. The order of subtraction for vector operations does affect the outcome (unlike scalar subtraction). Graphically, to find \(\vec{A} - \vec{B}\), you draw vector \(-\vec{B}\) from the head of \(\vec{A}\) and then connect the tail of \(\vec{A}\) to the head of \(-\vec{B}\). The resulting vector is the difference \(\vec{A} - \vec{B}\).

Example 1: Subtracting displacement vectors

A boat's engine pushes it North with a velocity of \(10 \text{ m/s}\) (\(\vec{V_E}\)). The current, however, flows South with a velocity of \(2 \text{ m/s}\) (\(\vec{V_C}\)). What is the boat's effective velocity relative to the shore, represented as \(\vec{V_E} - \vec{V_C}\)?

Solution:

To find \(\vec{V_E} - \vec{V_C}\), we need to add \(\vec{V_E}\) to the negative of \(\vec{V_C}\). The negative of \(\vec{V_C}\) (\(-\vec{V_C}\)) would be a velocity of \(2 \text{ m/s}\) North.

\(\vec{V_E} - \vec{V_C} = \vec{V_E} + (-\vec{V_C})\)

Since both \(\vec{V_E}\) and \(-\vec{V_C}\) are now in the same direction (North), we add their magnitudes:

\(|\vec{R}| = |10 \text{ m/s}| + |2 \text{ m/s}| = 12 \text{ m/s}\)

The resultant velocity is \(12 \text{ m/s}\) North. This represents a scenario where the current *helps* the boat, which is likely not what was intended by \(\vec{V_E} - \vec{V_C}\) in this context. If the question implies the current opposing the engine, it would typically be formulated as \(\vec{V_E} + \vec{V_{current}}\) where \(\vec{V_{current}}\) is South. However, strictly following the subtraction definition: \(\vec{V_E} - \vec{V_C}\) becomes \(\vec{V_E} + \text{ (South reversed to North)}\).

Example 2: Subtracting forces

A box is being pushed with a force of \(F_1 = 50 \text{ N}\) to the right. Another force \(F_2 = 30 \text{ N}\) is exerted on it, also to the right. If we want to find the difference \(F_1 - F_2\), what is the result?

Solution:

Subtracting \(F_2\) from \(F_1\) means adding \(F_1\) to the negative of \(F_2\). The negative of \(F_2\) (\(-F_2\)) would be a force of \(30 \text{ N}\) to the left.

\(F_1 - F_2 = F_1 + (-F_2)\)

Now we have \(50 \text{ N}\) to the right and \(30 \text{ N}\) to the left. Since they are in opposite directions, we subtract their magnitudes and take the direction of the larger force.

\(|\vec{R}| = |50 \text{ N}| - |30 \text{ N}| = 20 \text{ N}\)

The resultant force is \(20 \text{ N}\) to the right.

Summary of Vector Addition and Subtraction:

  • Vector addition is the process of finding the resultant vector, which is a single vector whose effect is the same as multiple individual vectors acting together.
  • Vector subtraction is equivalent to adding the negative of a vector. The negative of a vector has the same magnitude but the opposite direction.

2.2.4 Graphical Method of Vector Addition

Vectors can be added or subtracted using either algebraic or graphical methods. In this section, we'll focus on the graphical method, which involves drawing vectors to scale and determining the resultant using tools like a ruler and protractor.

The primary graphical techniques for vector addition include the triangle method, the parallelogram method, and the polygon method.

Procedure for using graphical method of vector addition:

  • Decide on an appropriate scale and record it on the diagram.
  • Choose a starting point.
  • Draw the first vector with its appropriate length and in the indicated direction.
  • Draw the second and any remaining vectors from the head of the previous vector with their appropriate length and direction.
  • Draw the resultant vector according to the specific rule being used (e.g., tail of the first to head of the last for triangle/polygon).
  • Measure the length of the resultant vector and use the scale to convert it to the magnitude of the resultant.
  • Use a protractor to measure the vector's direction.

i. Triangle Method of Vector Addition

The triangle law of vector addition is used for finding the sum of two vectors. This method, also known as the head-to-tail method, involves placing the tail of the second vector at the head of the first vector. The resultant vector is then found by drawing a line from the tail of the first vector to the head of the second vector, forming a triangle.

Specifically, if two vectors acting simultaneously on an object can be represented in both magnitude and direction by two sides of a triangle taken in order, then their resultant vector (both magnitude and direction) is given by the third side of that triangle taken in the opposite order.

Consider two vectors \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\). To add them using the triangle method, join the head of vector \(\vec{A}\) to the tail of vector \(\vec{B}\). The resultant vector \(\vec{R}\) will have its tail at the tail of \(\vec{A}\) and its head at the head of \(\vec{B}\). Mathematically:

\(\vec{R} = \vec{A} + \vec{B}\)

Example 1: Displacement by Triangle Method

A person walks \(4 \text{ km}\) East and then turns to walk \(3 \text{ km}\) North. Find their resultant displacement using the triangle method.

Solution:

  1. Scale: Let \(1 \text{ cm} = 1 \text{ km}\).
  2. Draw first vector: Draw a \(4 \text{ cm}\) arrow horizontally to the right (East), representing \(\vec{A}\).
  3. Draw second vector: From the arrowhead of \(\vec{A}\), draw a \(3 \text{ cm}\) arrow vertically upwards (North), representing \(\vec{B}\).
  4. Draw resultant: Draw a vector from the tail of \(\vec{A}\) to the head of \(\vec{B}\). This is \(\vec{R}\).
  5. Measure: Using a ruler, measure the length of \(\vec{R}\). It should be approximately \(5 \text{ cm}\). Using the scale, this is \(5 \text{ km}\). Using a protractor, measure the angle \(\vec{R}\) makes with the East direction. It should be approximately \(36.9^\circ\) North of East.

So, the resultant displacement is approximately \(5 \text{ km}\) at \(36.9^\circ\) North of East.

Example 2: Force addition by Triangle Method

A boat is pulled by two ropes. One rope exerts a force of \(80 \text{ N}\) directly North. The other rope exerts a force of \(60 \text{ N}\) directly East. Find the resultant force on the boat using the triangle method.

Solution:

  1. Scale: Let \(1 \text{ cm} = 20 \text{ N}\).
  2. Draw first vector: Draw a \(4 \text{ cm}\) arrow vertically upwards (North), representing \(\vec{F_1}\). (Since \(80 \text{ N} / 20 \text{ N/cm} = 4 \text{ cm}\)).
  3. Draw second vector: From the arrowhead of \(\vec{F_1}\), draw a \(3 \text{ cm}\) arrow horizontally to the right (East), representing \(\vec{F_2}\). (Since \(60 \text{ N} / 20 \text{ N/cm} = 3 \text{ cm}\)).
  4. Draw resultant: Draw a vector from the tail of \(\vec{F_1}\) to the head of \(\vec{F_2}\). This is \(\vec{R}\).
  5. Measure: Measure the length of \(\vec{R}\) (should be \(5 \text{ cm}\), representing \(100 \text{ N}\)) and its angle from the North direction (should be approximately \(36.9^\circ\) East of North).

The resultant force is approximately \(100 \text{ N}\) at \(36.9^\circ\) East of North.

ii. Parallelogram Method of Vector Addition

The parallelogram law of vector addition is used when two vectors are represented as the two adjacent sides of a parallelogram, with their tails joined at the same point. The sum of these two vectors is then given by the diagonal of the parallelogram that starts from the same common tail point.

If two vectors are represented by two adjacent sides (both in magnitude and direction) of a parallelogram drawn from a single point, their resultant vector is completely represented by the diagonal of the parallelogram drawn from that same point.

Suppose you have two vectors \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\). To add them using this method: first, translate one vector parallel to itself so that both vectors have their origins (tails) at the same point. Then, from the head of vector \(\vec{A}\), draw a line parallel to vector \(\vec{B}\). Similarly, from the head of vector \(\vec{B}\), draw a line parallel to vector \(\vec{A}\). This creates a parallelogram. The diagonal drawn from the common origin of the two vectors to the opposite corner of the parallelogram represents the resultant vector \(\vec{R}\).

\(\vec{R} = \vec{A} + \vec{B}\)

Example 1: Displacement by Parallelogram Method

A boat travels \(5 \text{ km}\) due East and then \(3 \text{ km}\) due North from the same starting point. Determine the resultant displacement using the parallelogram method.

Solution:

  1. Scale: Let \(1 \text{ cm} = 1 \text{ km}\).
  2. Draw vectors from common origin: Draw a \(5 \text{ cm}\) arrow to the East (\(\vec{A}\)) and a \(3 \text{ cm}\) arrow to the North (\(\vec{B}\)), both starting from the same point.
  3. Complete the parallelogram: From the head of \(\vec{A}\), draw a dashed line \(3 \text{ cm}\) long to the North (parallel to \(\vec{B}\)). From the head of \(\vec{B}\), draw a dashed line \(5 \text{ cm}\) long to the East (parallel to \(\vec{A}\)). These dashed lines will meet, forming a parallelogram.
  4. Draw resultant: Draw the diagonal from the common origin of \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\) to the opposite corner of the parallelogram. This is \(\vec{R}\).
  5. Measure: Measure the length of \(\vec{R}\) (should be \(5.83 \text{ cm}\), representing \(5.83 \text{ km}\)) and its angle from the East direction (approximately \(30.96^\circ\) North of East).

The resultant displacement is approximately \(5.83 \text{ km}\) at \(30.96^\circ\) North of East.

Example 2: Force addition by Parallelogram Method

Two forces act on an object at a single point. One force is \(60 \text{ N}\) acting horizontally to the right, and the other is \(40 \text{ N}\) acting vertically upwards. Find the resultant force using the parallelogram method.

Solution:

  1. Scale: Let \(1 \text{ cm} = 10 \text{ N}\).
  2. Draw vectors from common origin: Draw a \(6 \text{ cm}\) arrow horizontally right (\(\vec{F_1}\)) and a \(4 \text{ cm}\) arrow vertically up (\(\vec{F_2}\)), both originating from the same point.
  3. Complete the parallelogram: Draw a dashed line from the head of \(\vec{F_1}\) parallel to \(\vec{F_2}\). Draw another dashed line from the head of \(\vec{F_2}\) parallel to \(\vec{F_1}\).
  4. Draw resultant: Draw the diagonal from the common origin to the intersection of the dashed lines. This is \(\vec{R}\).
  5. Measure: Measure the length of \(\vec{R}\) (should be \(7.21 \text{ cm}\), representing \(72.1 \text{ N}\)). Measure the angle \(\vec{R}\) makes with the horizontal (\(33.69^\circ\) above horizontal).

The resultant force is approximately \(72.1 \text{ N}\) at \(33.69^\circ\) above the horizontal.

Vector addition is commutative, meaning the order of addition doesn't change the result:

\(\vec{A} + \vec{B} = \vec{B} + \vec{A}\)

iii. Polygon Method of Vector Addition

The polygon method is used for adding more than two vectors. In this method, you place the tail of each successive vector at the head of the previous one. The resultant of all vectors is then obtained by drawing a vector from the tail of the very first vector to the head of the very last vector.

Suppose you want to find the resultant vector \(\vec{R}\) of four vectors \(\vec{A}\), \(\vec{B}\), \(\vec{C}\), and \(\vec{D}\). You start by selecting any one of the vectors as the first. Then, you perform parallel translation to place the tail ("origin") of the second vector at the head ("end") of the first vector. You repeat this process for the third and subsequent vectors until all vectors are arranged head-to-tail. Finally, you draw the resultant vector \(\vec{R}\) by connecting the tail of the first vector to the head of the last vector. The head of the resultant vector will be at the head of the last vector.

For example, the resultant vector \(\vec{R}\) from \(\vec{D}\) to \(\vec{B}\) would be \(\vec{R} = \vec{D} + \vec{A} + \vec{C} + \vec{B}\). Because vector addition is associative and commutative, you will get the same resultant vector regardless of the order in which you add the vectors.

Note: The same procedure is followed during the subtraction of vectors, by using the negative of the vector to be subtracted.

Example 1: Multiple Displacements by Polygon Method

A hiker takes three successive displacements: \(\vec{D_1} = 5 \text{ km}\) East, \(\vec{D_2} = 3 \text{ km}\) North, and \(\vec{D_3} = 4 \text{ km}\) West. Find the resultant displacement using the polygon method.

Solution:

  1. Scale: Let \(1 \text{ cm} = 1 \text{ km}\).
  2. Draw \(\vec{D_1}\): Draw a \(5 \text{ cm}\) arrow to the East.
  3. Draw \(\vec{D_2}\) (head-to-tail): From the head of \(\vec{D_1}\), draw a \(3 \text{ cm}\) arrow to the North.
  4. Draw \(\vec{D_3}\) (head-to-tail): From the head of \(\vec{D_2}\), draw a \(4 \text{ cm}\) arrow to the West.
  5. Draw resultant: Draw a vector from the tail of \(\vec{D_1}\) to the head of \(\vec{D_3}\). This is \(\vec{R}\).
  6. Measure: The resultant vector will be \(1 \text{ km}\) East and \(3 \text{ km}\) North from the start. Using the Pythagorean theorem, \(|\vec{R}| = \sqrt{(1 \text{ km})^2 + (3 \text{ km})^2}\) \(= \sqrt{1 + 9} = \sqrt{10} \approx 3.16 \text{ km}\). The angle would be \(\tan^{-1}(3/1) \approx 71.57^\circ\) North of East.

The resultant displacement is approximately \(3.16 \text{ km}\) at \(71.57^\circ\) North of East.

Example 2: Forces on an object by Polygon Method

An object is subjected to three forces: \(\vec{F_1} = 10 \text{ N}\) at \(0^\circ\) (East), \(\vec{F_2} = 8 \text{ N}\) at \(90^\circ\) (North), and \(\vec{F_3} = 5 \text{ N}\) at \(180^\circ\) (West). Find the resultant force using the polygon method.

Solution:

  1. Scale: Let \(1 \text{ cm} = 2 \text{ N}\).
  2. Draw \(\vec{F_1}\): Draw a \(5 \text{ cm}\) arrow to the East. (\(10 \text{ N} / 2 \text{ N/cm} = 5 \text{ cm}\)).
  3. Draw \(\vec{F_2}\): From the head of \(\vec{F_1}\), draw a \(4 \text{ cm}\) arrow to the North. (\(8 \text{ N} / 2 \text{ N/cm} = 4 \text{ cm}\)).
  4. Draw \(\vec{F_3}\): From the head of \(\vec{F_2}\), draw a \(2.5 \text{ cm}\) arrow to the West. (\(5 \text{ N} / 2 \text{ N/cm} = 2.5 \text{ cm}\)).
  5. Draw resultant: Draw a vector from the tail of \(\vec{F_1}\) to the head of \(\vec{F_3}\). This is \(\vec{R}\).
  6. Measure: The net horizontal displacement is \(5 \text{ cm} - 2.5 \text{ cm} = 2.5 \text{ cm}\) East. The vertical displacement is \(4 \text{ cm}\) North. Using Pythagorean theorem, \(|\vec{R}|\) \(= \sqrt{(2.5 \text{ cm})^2 + (4 \text{ cm})^2}\) \(= \sqrt{6.25 + 16} = \sqrt{22.25} \approx 4.72 \text{ cm}\). In terms of force, \(|\vec{R}| \approx 4.72 \text{ cm} \times 2 \text{ N/cm} = 9.44 \text{ N}\). The angle is \(\tan^{-1}(4/2.5) \approx 57.99^\circ\) North of East.

The resultant force is approximately \(9.44 \text{ N}\) at \(57.99^\circ\) North of East.

Let's consider a few special cases for vector addition:

  1. When the two vectors are in the same direction (parallel): If vectors \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\) are parallel, the magnitude of the resultant vector \(\vec{R}\) is simply the sum of their individual magnitudes.

    \(|\vec{R}| = |\vec{A}| + |\vec{B}|\)

    The direction of the resultant vector will be the same as the direction of the individual vectors.
  2. When the two vectors are acting in opposite directions (anti-parallel): If vectors \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\) are anti-parallel (i.e., in opposite directions), the magnitude of the resultant vector \(\vec{R}\) is the difference between their magnitudes.

    \(|\vec{R}| = ||\vec{A}| - |\vec{B}|| \)

    The direction of the resultant vector will be in the direction of the vector with the larger magnitude.

    To determine the resultant of two vectors:

    • If acting in the same direction, add their magnitudes and keep the common direction.
    • If acting in opposite directions, find the difference in their magnitudes and assign the direction of the vector with the greater value.

    Note: The resultant of two vectors acting at the same point is at its maximum when the vectors act in the same direction and at its minimum when they act in opposite directions.

  3. When the two vectors are perpendicular to each other: If vectors \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\) are perpendicular, the magnitude of the resultant vector \(\vec{R}\) is found using the Pythagorean theorem:

    \(|\vec{R}| = \sqrt{|\vec{A}|^2 + |\vec{B}|^2}\)

    The direction of the resultant vector can be found using the trigonometric tangent function:

    \(\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{|\vec{B}|}{|\vec{A}|}\right)\)

    Where \(\theta\) is the angle the resultant makes with vector \(\vec{A}\). You can compare these results with those obtained by direct measurement with a ruler and protractor; they should be similar.

Example 1: Resultant for perpendicular vectors

Two forces, \(F_1 = 30 \text{ N}\) acting East and \(F_2 = 40 \text{ N}\) acting North, are applied to an object. What is the magnitude and direction of the resultant force?

Solution:

Since the forces are perpendicular, we use the Pythagorean theorem for magnitude:

\(|\vec{R}| = \sqrt{(30 \text{ N})^2 + (40 \text{ N})^2}\) \(= \sqrt{900 + 1600} = \sqrt{2500} = 50 \text{ N}\)

For direction, we use the tangent function:

\(\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{40 \text{ N}}{30 \text{ N}}\right) = \tan^{-1}(1.333) \approx 53.1^\circ\)

The resultant force is \(50 \text{ N}\) at \(53.1^\circ\) North of East.

Example 2: Resultant of Two Perpendicular Velocities

An airplane flies with a velocity of \(200 \text{ km/h}\) North. A crosswind blows with a velocity of \(150 \text{ km/h}\) East. What is the resultant velocity of the airplane?

Solution:

The two velocity vectors are perpendicular. We find the magnitude using the Pythagorean theorem:

\(|\vec{R}| = \sqrt{(200 \text{ km/h})^2 + (150 \text{ km/h})^2}\) \(= \sqrt{40000 + 22500} = \sqrt{62500} = 250 \text{ km/h}\)

For the direction:

\(\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{150 \text{ km/h}}{200 \text{ km/h}}\right) = \tan^{-1}(0.75) \approx 36.9^\circ\)

The resultant velocity is \(250 \text{ km/h}\) at \(36.9^\circ\) East of North (or \(53.1^\circ\) North of East).

Summary of Graphical Methods:

  • Two vectors can be added graphically using the triangle method or the parallelogram method.
  • For adding more than two vectors, the polygon method is used.

2.2.5 Vector Resolution

Just as multiple vectors can be combined to form a single resultant vector, a single vector can also be broken down into several component vectors that, when added together, give back the original vector. This process is called resolving a vector into its components.

Resolving a vector into components means breaking down a single vector into a set of other vectors (its components) that, when added, produce the original vector. These components are usually chosen along perpendicular axes (like x and y axes).

Placing vectors within a chosen coordinate system makes it possible to decompose them into components along each axis. In a rectangular coordinate system, a vector \(\vec{A}\) can be resolved into two component vectors: \(\vec{A_x}\), which is parallel to the x-axis, and \(\vec{A_y}\), which is parallel to the y-axis.

The horizontal and vertical components of a vector can be found using two methods: the graphical method and the trigonometric method.

i. Graphical Method of Vector Resolution:

Here are the steps to resolve a vector graphically:

  1. Choose a scale and draw the vector accurately in the appropriate direction.
  2. Extend the x- and y-axes from the tail of the vector to encompass the entire length of the vector.
  3. From the arrowhead of the vector, draw perpendicular lines (projections) to both the x- and y-axes.
  4. Draw the x-component (\(\vec{A_x}\)) from the tail of the vector to the intersection point on the x-axis created by the perpendicular projection.
  5. Draw the y-component (\(\vec{A_y}\)) from the tail of the vector to the intersection point on the y-axis created by the perpendicular projection.
  6. Measure the lengths of these two components and use your chosen scale to determine their magnitudes.

Example 1: Resolving a Displacement Vector Graphically

A bird flies \(10 \text{ m}\) at an angle of \(30^\circ\) above the horizontal. Resolve this displacement into its horizontal (\(D_x\)) and vertical (\(D_y\)) components graphically.

Solution:

  1. Scale: Let \(1 \text{ cm} = 2 \text{ m}\). So, \(10 \text{ m}\) will be a \(5 \text{ cm}\) arrow.
  2. Draw vector: Draw a \(5 \text{ cm}\) arrow at \(30^\circ\) above the horizontal from the origin.
  3. Draw projections: From the head of the arrow, drop a perpendicular line to the horizontal axis (x-axis) and another perpendicular line to the vertical axis (y-axis).
  4. Draw components: Draw a vector from the origin to where the perpendicular meets the x-axis (\(D_x\)). Draw another vector from the origin to where the perpendicular meets the y-axis (\(D_y\)).
  5. Measure:
    • Measure \(D_x\). It should be approximately \(4.33 \text{ cm}\), which corresponds to \(8.66 \text{ m}\).
    • Measure \(D_y\). It should be approximately \(2.5 \text{ cm}\), which corresponds to \(5 \text{ m}\).

So, the horizontal component is about \(8.66 \text{ m}\) and the vertical component is about \(5 \text{ m}\).

Example 2: Resolving a Force Vector Graphically

A force of \(50 \text{ N}\) is applied to a box at an angle of \(60^\circ\) with the horizontal. Find its horizontal (\(F_x\)) and vertical (\(F_y\)) components graphically.

Solution:

  1. Scale: Let \(1 \text{ cm} = 10 \text{ N}\). So, \(50 \text{ N}\) will be a \(5 \text{ cm}\) arrow.
  2. Draw vector: Draw a \(5 \text{ cm}\) arrow at \(60^\circ\) above the horizontal from the origin.
  3. Draw projections: From the head of the force vector, draw perpendiculars to the horizontal (x) and vertical (y) axes.
  4. Draw components: Draw \(F_x\) from the origin to the x-axis projection and \(F_y\) from the origin to the y-axis projection.
  5. Measure:
    • Measure \(F_x\). It should be approximately \(2.5 \text{ cm}\), corresponding to \(25 \text{ N}\).
    • Measure \(F_y\). It should be approximately \(4.33 \text{ cm}\), corresponding to \(43.3 \text{ N}\).

The horizontal component is about \(25 \text{ N}\) and the vertical component is about \(43.3 \text{ N}\).

ii. Trigonometric Method of Vector Resolution:

The trigonometric method relies on the definitions of sine, cosine, and tangent functions. Using trigonometry, the components can be calculated when the angle \(\theta\) is measured counterclockwise from the positive x-axis:

Horizontal component (\(A_x\)):

\(\cos \theta = \frac{\text{Adjacent side}}{\text{hypotenuse}} = \frac{A_x}{|\vec{A}|} \implies A_x = |\vec{A}|\cos \theta\)

Vertical component (\(A_y\)):

\(\sin \theta = \frac{\text{Opposite side}}{\text{hypotenuse}} = \frac{A_y}{|\vec{A}|} \implies A_y = |\vec{A}|\sin \theta\)

The original vector is the sum of its two component vectors:

\(\vec{A} = \vec{A_x} + \vec{A_y}\)

Since \(\vec{A_x}\) and \(\vec{A_y}\) are at a right angle (\(90^\circ\)), the magnitude of the resultant vector (which is the original vector \(\vec{A}\)) can be calculated using the Pythagorean theorem:

\(|\vec{A}| = \sqrt{A_x^2 + A_y^2}\)

To find the angle or direction of the resultant vector (the original vector \(\vec{A}\)), the tangent of the angle it makes with the x-axis is given by:

\(\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{A_y}{A_x}\right)\)

where \(\tan \theta = \frac{\text{Opposite side}}{\text{Adjacent side}} = \frac{A_y}{A_x}\).

Example 1: Resolving a Displacement

A motorist drives \(250 \text{ km}\) in a direction \(30^\circ\) North of East. Resolve this displacement into its East (\(D_E\)) and North (\(D_N\)) components.

Solution:

Here, the magnitude \(|\vec{D}| = 250 \text{ km}\) and \(\theta = 30^\circ\).

North component (\(D_N\)):

\(D_N = |\vec{D}|\sin \theta = (250 \text{ km})(\sin 30^\circ)\) \(= (250 \text{ km})(0.5) = 125 \text{ km}\)

East component (\(D_E\)):

\(D_E = |\vec{D}|\cos \theta = (250 \text{ km})(\cos 30^\circ)\) \(= (250 \text{ km})(0.866) \approx 216.5 \text{ km}\)

So, the displacement has a North component of \(125 \text{ km}\) and an East component of approximately \(216.5 \text{ km}\).

Example 2: Resolving a Force

A force of \(100 \text{ N}\) is applied to an object at an angle of \(45^\circ\) to the horizontal. Calculate its horizontal (\(F_x\)) and vertical (\(F_y\)) components.

Solution:

Here, magnitude \(|\vec{F}| = 100 \text{ N}\) and \(\theta = 45^\circ\).

Horizontal component (\(F_x\)):

\(F_x = |\vec{F}|\cos \theta = (100 \text{ N})(\cos 45^\circ)\) \(= (100 \text{ N})(0.707) \approx 70.7 \text{ N}\)

Vertical component (\(F_y\)):

\(F_y = |\vec{F}|\sin \theta = (100 \text{ N})(\sin 45^\circ)\) \(= (100 \text{ N})(0.707) \approx 70.7 \text{ N}\)

Thus, the horizontal component of the force is approximately \(70.7 \text{ N}\), and the vertical component is also approximately \(70.7 \text{ N}\).

Summary of Vector Resolution:

  • Any vector can be broken down into a horizontal and a vertical component.
  • The combined effect of these horizontal and vertical components is identical to the effect of the original vector.
  • When vector \(\vec{A}\) is resolved along a rectangular coordinate system, its horizontal component is \(A_x = |\vec{A}|\cos \theta\) and its vertical component is \(A_y = |\vec{A}|\sin \theta\), where \(\theta\) is the angle with the x-axis.

Some physical quantities like mass, speed, distance, and density can be fully described by their magnitude and units alone. These are called scalar quantities. However, many other quantities we encounter daily, such as force, velocity, acceleration, and displacement, require both their magnitude and direction for complete specification. These are known as vector quantities.

In Grade 10, you learned that a vector can be represented graphically by an arrow. The length of the arrow, drawn to scale, represents the magnitude of the vector, and the tip of the arrow indicates its direction. For example, a 10 km displacement might be represented by a 1 cm long vector, and a 20 km displacement by a 2 cm long vector. Similarly, a velocity of 5 m/s might be a 1 cm vector, while 40 m/s would be an 8 cm vector.

2.3.1 Types of Vectors

  • Parallel vectors: Vectors that point in the same direction are called parallel vectors.

  • Antiparallel vectors: Vectors that have opposite directions, regardless of their magnitudes, are called antiparallel vectors.

  • Equal vectors: If two vectors representing the same quantity have both the same magnitude and the same direction, they are considered equal vectors, regardless of their position in space.

  • Collinear vectors: These are vectors that lie along the same line or parallel lines. Two vectors are collinear if they are parallel to the same line, irrespective of their magnitudes and direction.

  • Coplanar vectors: Three or more vectors that lie in the same plane or are parallel to the same plane are known as coplanar vectors.

  • Zero vector: A zero vector has a magnitude of zero, and its starting point coincides with its terminal point.

  • Orthogonal vectors: Vectors that are perpendicular to one another.

  • Unit vector: A vector with a magnitude of unit length is called a unit vector.

The vector \(-\vec{A}\) is not equal to vector \(\vec{A}\) because its direction is opposite to that of \(\vec{A}\). We define the Negative of a vector as a vector having the same magnitude as the original vector but in the opposite direction. The negative of vector \(\vec{A}\) is denoted as \(-\vec{A}\).

Example 1: Finding the Negative of a Vector

Find the negative of each of the following vectors.

a. Displacement vector = 60 km toward North

b. Velocity vector = 100 km/h toward 200° Southeast

Solution

The negative of a vector has the same magnitude as the original vector but in the opposite direction.

a. If \(\vec{S}\) = 60 km, toward North, then \(-\vec{S}\) = 60 km, toward South.

b. If \(\vec{S}\) = 100 km/h, toward 200° Southeast, then \(-\vec{S}\) = 100 km/h, toward 200° Northwest.

2.3.2 Graphical Method of Addition of Vectors in Two Dimensions (2-D)

After completing this section, you should be able to:

  • define the term resultant vector.
  • explain the geometric method for addition and subtraction of vectors in a plane.
  • apply the geometric method of addition of vectors to find the resultant of vectors in two dimensions.

Graphical methods of vector addition provide a visual understanding of vectors and are commonly used in navigation. For instance, a trip starting at point A, going to point B, and then ending at point C. The total distance traveled is the sum of the magnitudes of the individual segments. However, the displacement from A to C is a single vector, known as the resultant displacement. We will discuss the Triangle Law, Parallelogram Law, and Polygon Law of vector addition.

When two or more vectors are added, they must have the same units. It wouldn't make sense to add a force vector (in Newtons) to a velocity vector (in meters per second). Similarly, scalars also obey this rule; you wouldn't add mass to volume. The graphical method of vector addition involves using equal vectors. A vector is not altered if it is displaced parallel to itself, as long as its length remains unchanged. This process is called translating a vector.

i. Triangle Law of Vector Addition

If a particle undergoes a displacement \(\vec{S_1}\) followed by a second displacement \(\vec{S_2}\), the final result is the same as if the particle had started at the same initial point and undergone a single displacement \(\vec{S}\). We call displacement \(\vec{S}\) the vector sum, or resultantstrong>, of displacements \(\vec{S_1}\) and \(\vec{S_2}\). We express this relationship symbolically as:

\(\vec{S} = \vec{S_1} + \vec{S_2}\)

This can be shown graphically by placing the vectors head-to-tail. The arrow drawn from the tail of the first vector to the head of the second vector represents the resultant. This is known as the Triangle Law of vector addition.

Example 1: Applying the Triangle Law

Imagine you walk 3 km East, then turn and walk 4 km North. What is your resultant displacement from your starting point?

Solution

Using the triangle law of vector addition, place the 3 km East vector (Vector A) horizontally. From the head of Vector A, place the tail of the 4 km North vector (Vector B) vertically upwards. The resultant vector (Vector R) will be drawn from the tail of Vector A to the head of Vector B.

Magnitude of R: This forms a right-angled triangle. We can use the Pythagorean theorem:

\(R^2 = A^2 + B^2\)

\(R^2 = (3 \, \text{km})^2 + (4 \, \text{km})^2\)

\(R^2 = 9 \, \text{km}^2 + 16 \, \text{km}^2\)

\(R^2 = 25 \, \text{km}^2\)

\(R = \sqrt{25 \, \text{km}^2} = 5 \, \text{km}\)

Direction of R: To find the direction, let \(\theta\) be the angle from the East direction.

\(\tan \theta = \frac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Adjacent}} = \frac{4 \, \text{km}}{3 \, \text{km}}\)

\(\tan \theta = 1.333\)

\(\theta = \arctan(1.333) \approx 53.1^\circ\)

So, the resultant displacement is 5 km at 53.1° North of East.

ii. Parallelogram Law of Vector Addition

Two vectors can also be added graphically by constructing a parallelogram using the vectors as its sides. The vectors are placed tail-to-tail, and the diagonal drawn from the tails of the vectors to the opposite corner of the parallelogram represents the resultant.

Example 1: Parallelogram Law

Two forces, \(\vec{F_1}\) of 5 N acting horizontally to the right and \(\vec{F_2}\) of 3 N acting at an angle of 60° above the horizontal, are applied to an object. Find the resultant force using the parallelogram law.

Solution

1. Draw \(\vec{F_1}\) from an origin point, representing 5 N horizontally.

2. From the same origin point, draw \(\vec{F_2}\), representing 3 N at 60° above the horizontal.

3. Complete the parallelogram by drawing a line parallel to \(\vec{F_1}\) from the head of \(\vec{F_2}\), and a line parallel to \(\vec{F_2}\) from the head of \(\vec{F_1}\). These lines will intersect at a point.

4. Draw the resultant vector \(\vec{R}\) from the origin (common tail of \(\vec{F_1}\) and \(\vec{F_2}\)) to the intersection point of the parallel lines. The length of this diagonal represents the magnitude of the resultant force, and its direction is the direction of the resultant.

By measuring the length of \(\vec{R}\) and the angle it makes with \(\vec{F_1}\), one can find the magnitude and direction. For instance, using the law of cosines (if angles are known):

\(R^2 = F_1^2 + F_2^2 - 2F_1F_2 \cos(180^\circ - \theta)\)

Here, \(\theta = 60^\circ\), so \((180^\circ - \theta) = 120^\circ\).

\(R^2 = 5^2 + 3^2 - 2(5)(3) \cos(120^\circ)\)

\(R^2 = 25 + 9 - 30(-0.5)\)

\(R^2 = 34 + 15\)

\(R^2 = 49\)

\(R = 7 \, \text{N}\)

The direction can be found using the law of sines.

iii. Polygon Law of Vector Addition

When more than two vectors need to be added, we can use the Polygon Law of Vector Addition. Start with one vector, then place the tail of the next vector at the head of the first, and continue this process until all vectors are included. The vector drawn from the tail of the first vector to the head of the last vector represents the resultant.

\(\vec{R} = \vec{A} + \vec{B} + \vec{C} + \vec{D}\)

Key Concepts

  • Scalar quantities are described by magnitude only (e.g., mass, speed).
  • Vector quantities are described by both magnitude and direction (e.g., force, velocity, displacement).
  • Vectors can be represented graphically by arrows. The length of the arrow indicates magnitude, and the arrowhead indicates direction.
  • The negative of a vector has the same magnitude but the opposite direction.
  • The resultant vector is the sum of two or more vectors.
  • Triangle Law: Vectors are placed head-to-tail, and the resultant connects the tail of the first to the head of the last.
  • Parallelogram Law: Vectors are placed tail-to-tail, and the resultant is the diagonal drawn from the common tail.
  • Polygon Law: Used for adding more than two vectors by placing them head-to-tail sequentially; the resultant connects the tail of the first to the head of the last.

2.3.3.1 Adding Two Collinear Vectors

Unlike scalars, which can be added or subtracted using simple arithmetic based only on their magnitudes, vector addition involves both magnitude and direction. Before we delve into adding vectors in two dimensions, let's review the fundamental principles of adding vectors in one dimension.

Consider two force vectors, \(\vec{F_1}\) and \(\vec{F_2}\), acting at a point in the same direction. The resultant vector \(\vec{R}\) will have a magnitude equal to the sum of the magnitudes of each vector and will be directed along the same direction as the individual vectors.

\(\vec{R} = \vec{F_1} + \vec{F_2}\)

When force vectors \(\vec{F_1}\) and \(\vec{F_2}\) are acting in opposite directions, their resultant \(\vec{R}\) will have a magnitude equal to the absolute value of the difference in magnitudes of the vectors. It will be directed along the direction of the larger vector.

Example 1: Collinear Vectors in the Same Direction

A man rows his boat along the direction of flow of a river (downstream). If the boat can sail in still water at 0.50 m/s, and the river flows at 0.30 m/s, what is the resultant velocity of the boat?

Solution

Let the magnitude of the velocity of the river be \(A = 0.30 \, \text{m/s}\).

Let the magnitude of the velocity of the boat in still water be \(B = 0.50 \, \text{m/s}\).

Since both vectors are along the same direction (downstream), the magnitude of the resultant velocity is given by:

\(R = A + B = 0.30 \, \text{m/s} + 0.50 \, \text{m/s}\)

\(R = 0.80 \, \text{m/s}\)

The direction of the resultant is along the direction of the river flow (downstream).

Therefore, the resultant velocity is 0.80 m/s, downstream.

Example 2: Collinear Vectors in Opposite Directions

Two men are pushing a block along a horizontal surface by exerting oppositely directed forces of magnitudes 200 N and 100 N, respectively. What is the resultant force applied on the block by the two men?

Solution

Let the magnitude of the force exerted by the man on the right be \(F_1 = 200 \, \text{N}\).

Let the magnitude of the force exerted by the man on the left be \(F_2 = 100 \, \text{N}\).

Since the forces are oppositely directed, we subtract their magnitudes. The direction of the resultant will be that of the larger force.

\(R = F_1 - F_2 = 200 \, \text{N} - 100 \, \text{N}\)

\(R = 100 \, \text{N}\)

The direction of the resultant is along the direction of the 200 N (larger) force, which is toward the right.

Therefore, the resultant force is 100 N, toward the right.

2.3.3.2 Adding Two Perpendicular Vectors

When vectors lie in a plane and are perpendicular to each other, the rules for addition become more sophisticated than for one-dimensional vectors. We use a graphical method to construct the resultant vector and then apply trigonometry to determine its magnitude and direction. For two perpendicular vectors, \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\), we use the Pythagorean theorem for the magnitude of the resultant and trigonometry to find its direction.

Recall (Pythagorean Theorem and Trigonometric Ratios)

According to the Pythagorean theorem, for a right-angled triangle, the square of the hypotenuse (\(c\)) is equal to the sum of the squares of the other two sides (\(a\) and \(b\)):

\(c^2 = a^2 + b^2\)

\(c = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}\)

The trigonometric ratios are defined as:

\(\sin \theta = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}} = \frac{b}{c}\)

\(\cos \theta = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}} = \frac{a}{c}\)

\(\tan \theta = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}} = \frac{b}{a}\)

Example 1: Perpendicular Displacement

An ant, starting at a point, walked a distance of 40 cm due South and then 50 cm due West. What is the resultant displacement of the ant from its starting point?

Solution

1. Represent the first displacement as \(\vec{S_1}\) = 40 cm, South.

2. Represent the second displacement as \(\vec{S_2}\) = 50 cm, West.

Since South and West are perpendicular directions, we can use the Pythagorean theorem to find the magnitude of the resultant displacement, \(\vec{S}\).

\(S = \sqrt{S_1^2 + S_2^2}\)

\(S = \sqrt{(40 \, \text{cm})^2 + (50 \, \text{cm})^2}\)

\(S = \sqrt{1600 \, \text{cm}^2 + 2500 \, \text{cm}^2}\)

\(S = \sqrt{4100 \, \text{cm}^2}\)

\(S \approx 64.0 \, \text{cm}\)

To find the direction, let \(\theta\) be the angle from the West axis toward the South. (Alternatively, from the South axis toward the West).

Using the angle from West toward South:

\(\tan \theta = \frac{\text{Opposite (South component)}}{\text{Adjacent (West component)}} = \frac{40 \, \text{cm}}{50 \, \text{cm}}\)

\(\tan \theta = 0.8\)

\(\theta = \arctan(0.8) \approx 38.7^\circ\)

Therefore, the resultant displacement is approximately 64.0 cm at 38.7° South of West.

2.3.3.3 Components of a Vector

The previous sections discussed vector addition using graphical methods (scale diagrams) and right-angled triangles. While graphical methods offer limited accuracy, and right-triangle calculations only work for perpendicular vectors, this section introduces a more general and simpler method for adding vectors: the Component Method.

In a rectangular (Cartesian) coordinate system, a vector \(\vec{A}\) with its tail at the origin can be broken down into its components. The projection of vector \(\vec{A}\) onto the x-axis is called its x-component (\(A_x\)), and its projection onto the y-axis is called its y-component (\(A_y\)). \(A_x\) and \(A_y\) are scalar numbers, not vectors. However, \(\vec{A_x}\) and \(\vec{A_y}\) are the vector components of \(\vec{A}\). In symbols, \(\vec{A} = \vec{A_x} + \vec{A_y}\).

The x and y components of vector \(\vec{A}\) have magnitudes of \(A_x = A \cos \theta\) and \(A_y = A \sin \theta\), respectively, where \(\theta\) is the angle the vector makes with the positive x-axis. Components can be positive or negative depending on the vector's orientation. The process of breaking a vector into its components is known as resolution of a vector.

Key Terms

  • Component of a vector: The projection of a vector onto a coordinate axis.
  • Resolution of a vector: The process of breaking a vector into its components.

Example 1: Resolving a Force Vector

A force vector \(\vec{F}\) of magnitude 35 N is pushing on a box placed on a horizontal ground. If the force makes an angle of 40° above the negative x-axis, what are the x and y components of the force?

Solution

The force \(\vec{F}\) can be placed on the xy-plane. Remember that translating a vector does not change its magnitude or direction.

The angle is 40° above the negative x-axis. This means the angle relative to the positive x-axis is \(180^\circ - 40^\circ = 140^\circ\).

Alternatively, we can work directly with the 40° angle and use appropriate signs based on the quadrant.

For the x-component (\(F_x\)): Since the force is directed towards the negative x-axis side, \(F_x\) will be negative.

\(F_x = F \cos(180^\circ - 40^\circ) = F \cos(140^\circ)\)

\(F_x = 35 \, \text{N} \times (-0.766) \approx -26.8 \, \text{N}\)

For the y-component (\(F_y\)): Since the force is directed above the x-axis, \(F_y\) will be positive.

\(F_y = F \sin(180^\circ - 40^\circ) = F \sin(140^\circ)\)

\(F_y = 35 \, \text{N} \times (0.643) \approx 22.5 \, \text{N}\)

So, the components are approximately \(F_x = -26.8 \, \text{N}\) and \(F_y = 22.5 \, \text{N}\).

2.3.3.4 Finding the Resultant of Vectors by Component Method

To determine the resultant of two or more vectors using the component method, we resolve each vector into its x and y components. The x-component of the resultant (\(R_x\)) will be the sum of the x-components of the individual vectors, and the y-component of the resultant (\(R_y\)) will be the sum of the y-components of the individual vectors.

In terms of vector components, we write:

\(\vec{R} = \vec{R_x} + \vec{R_y}\)

Where \(\vec{R_x} = \vec{A_x} + \vec{B_x}\) and \(\vec{R_y} = \vec{A_y} + \vec{B_y}\)

The magnitude of the resultant vector will be:

\(R = \sqrt{R_x^2 + R_y^2}\)

The direction of the resultant is given by the angle \(\theta\) it makes with the x-axis:

\(\tan \theta = \frac{R_y}{R_x}\)

So, \(\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{R_y}{R_x}\right)\)

  • If both \(R_x\) and \(R_y\) are positive, angle \(\theta\) is above the +x axis (Quadrant I).
  • If \(R_x\) is negative and \(R_y\) is positive, angle \(\theta\) is above the -x axis (Quadrant II).
  • If both \(R_x\) and \(R_y\) are negative, angle \(\theta\) is below the -x axis (Quadrant III).
  • If \(R_x\) is positive and \(R_y\) is negative, angle \(\theta\) is below the +x axis (Quadrant IV).

Example 1: Resultant Displacement by Components

Consider two displacement vectors \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\) of magnitudes 100 m and 200 m respectively. Vector \(\vec{A}\) makes an angle of 50° with the positive x-axis, and vector \(\vec{B}\) makes an angle of 30° below the negative x-axis. Find the magnitude and direction of the resultant displacement vector.

Solution

First, resolve the vectors into their x and y components.

For vector \(\vec{A}\):

  • \(A_x = A \cos 50^\circ = 100 \, \text{m} \times (0.643) = 64.3 \, \text{m}\)
  • \(A_y = A \sin 50^\circ = 100 \, \text{m} \times (0.766) = 76.6 \, \text{m}\)

For vector \(\vec{B}\): The angle is 30° below the negative x-axis. This means the x-component is negative, and the y-component is negative.

  • \(B_x = -B \cos 30^\circ = -200 \, \text{m} \times (0.866)\) \(= -173.2 \, \text{m}\)
  • \(B_y = -B \sin 30^\circ = -200 \, \text{m} \times (0.50)\) \(= -100 \, \text{m}\)

Now, calculate the components of the resultant vector \(\vec{R}\):

  • \(R_x = A_x + B_x = 64.3 \, \text{m} + (-173.2 \, \text{m})\) \(= -108.9 \, \text{m}\)
  • \(R_y = A_y + B_y = 76.6 \, \text{m} + (-100 \, \text{m})\) \(= -23.4 \, \text{m}\)

The magnitude of the resultant is:

\(R = \sqrt{R_x^2 + R_y^2}\) \(= \sqrt{(-108.9 \, \text{m})^2 + (-23.4 \, \text{m})^2}\)

\(R = \sqrt{11859.21 + 547.56}\) \(= \sqrt{12406.77} \approx 111.4 \, \text{m}\)

The direction is given by:

\(\tan \theta = \frac{R_y}{R_x} = \frac{-23.4 \, \text{m}}{-108.9 \, \text{m}} \approx 0.215\)

\(\theta = \tan^{-1}(0.215) \approx 12.1^\circ\)

Since both \(R_x\) and \(R_y\) are negative, the resultant vector is in the third quadrant. The angle 12.1° is measured from the negative x-axis towards the negative y-axis. Therefore, the direction can be stated as 12.1° South of West, or \(180^\circ + 12.1^\circ = 192.1^\circ\) from the positive x-axis.

So, the resultant displacement is approximately 111.4 m at 12.1° South of West (or 192.1° from the positive x-axis).

2.3.3.5 Unit Vectors

As previously discussed, a unit vector is a vector of magnitude one, with no unit. Unit vectors are used to represent the direction of vectors in space. The symbol for unit vectors is denoted by a 'hat' (e.g., \(\hat{i}\), \(\hat{j}\)) to distinguish them from ordinary vectors.

In an xy-coordinate system, we define:

  • \(\hat{i}\) as a unit vector pointing in the direction of the positive x-axis.
  • \(\hat{j}\) as a unit vector pointing in the direction of the positive y-axis.

Vector components of a vector \(\vec{A}\) and its components are related as:

  • \(\vec{A_x} = A_x \hat{i}\)
  • \(\vec{A_y} = A_y \hat{j}\)

Vector \(\vec{A}\) can be written in vector equation as:

\(\vec{A} = A_x \hat{i} + A_y \hat{j}\)

Using unit vectors, the resultant \(\vec{R}\) of two vectors \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\) can be expressed as:

\(\vec{A} = A_x \hat{i} + A_y \hat{j}\)

\(\vec{B} = B_x \hat{i} + B_y \hat{j}\)

\(\vec{R} = \vec{A} + \vec{B}\)

\(\vec{R} = (A_x \hat{i} + A_y \hat{j}) + (B_x \hat{i} + B_y \hat{j})\)

\(\vec{R} = (A_x + B_x) \hat{i} + (A_y + B_y) \hat{j}\)

\(\vec{R} = R_x \hat{i} + R_y \hat{j}\)

The unit vector along the direction of a given non-zero vector is defined as the vector divided by its magnitude.

If vector \(\vec{A} = A_x \hat{i} + A_y \hat{j}\), then the unit vector along \(\vec{A}\) is:

\(\hat{u}_A = \frac{\vec{A}}{A}\)

Example 1: Unit Vector for a Force

Given force vector \(\vec{F} = (12\hat{i} - 16\hat{j})\) N, what is a unit vector along \(\vec{F}\)?

Solution

First, find the magnitude of vector \(\vec{F}\):

\(F = \sqrt{F_x^2 + F_y^2} = \sqrt{(12)^2 + (-16)^2}\)

\(F = \sqrt{144 + 256} = \sqrt{400}\)

\(F = 20 \, \text{N}\)

Now, divide the vector by its magnitude to find the unit vector \(\hat{u}_F\):

\(\hat{u}_F = \frac{\vec{F}}{F} = \frac{12\hat{i} - 16\hat{j}}{20}\)

\(\hat{u}_F = \frac{12}{20}\hat{i} - \frac{16}{20}\hat{j}\)

\(\hat{u}_F = (0.6\hat{i} - 0.8\hat{j})\)

To verify, the magnitude of \(\hat{u}_F\) should be 1:

\(|\hat{u}_F| = \sqrt{(0.6)^2 + (-0.8)^2}\) \(= \sqrt{0.36 + 0.64} = \sqrt{1} = 1\)

Example 2: Resultant of Two Vectors with Unit Vectors

Vector \(\vec{A}\) has x and y components of 4 units and 2 units, respectively. Vector \(\vec{B}\) has corresponding components of -9 units and 3 units, respectively. Find:

a) The vector components of their resultant.

b) The magnitude and direction of their resultant.

Solution

Given:

\(\vec{A} = (4\hat{i} + 2\hat{j})\) units

\(\vec{B} = (-9\hat{i} + 3\hat{j})\) units

a) The resultant \(\vec{R} = \vec{A} + \vec{B}\):

\(\vec{R} = (4\hat{i} + 2\hat{j}) + (-9\hat{i} + 3\hat{j})\)

\(\vec{R} = (4 - 9)\hat{i} + (2 + 3)\hat{j}\)

\(\vec{R} = (-5\hat{i} + 5\hat{j})\) units

The x-component of the resultant is \(R_x = -5\) units, and the y-component is \(R_y = 5\) units.

b) The magnitude of the resultant \(\vec{R}\):

\(R = \sqrt{R_x^2 + R_y^2} = \sqrt{(-5)^2 + (5)^2}\)

\(R = \sqrt{25 + 25} = \sqrt{50} = 5\sqrt{2}\) units

The direction of the resultant \(\vec{R}\):

\(\tan \theta = \frac{R_y}{R_x} = \frac{5}{-5} = -1\)

\(\theta = \tan^{-1}(-1)\)

Since \(R_x\) is negative and \(R_y\) is positive, the vector is in the second quadrant. The reference angle is \(45^\circ\). Therefore, the angle from the positive x-axis is \(180^\circ - 45^\circ = 135^\circ\).

The resultant is \(5\sqrt{2}\) units at 135° from the positive x-axis (or 45° above the negative x-axis).

Example 1

An unknown vector \(\vec{D}\) is added to vector \(\vec{C} = (-4\hat{i} + 5\hat{j})\) units and the resultant \(\vec{R} = \vec{C} + \vec{D}\) has x and y components of each -1 and 1 units, respectively. Find the magnitude of the unknown vector.

Solution

Given \(\vec{C} = (-4\hat{i} + 5\hat{j})\), \(\vec{D} = (D_x\hat{i} + D_y\hat{j})\) and \(\vec{R} = (-\hat{i} + \hat{j})\)

For \(\vec{R} = \vec{C} + \vec{D}\), we have:

\(R_x = C_x + D_x\)

\(-1 = -4 + D_x \Rightarrow D_x = 3 \text{ units}\)

\(R_y = C_y + D_y\)

\(1 = 5 + D_y \Rightarrow D_y = -4 \text{ units}\)

Vector \(\vec{D} = (3\hat{i} - 4\hat{j}) \text{ units}\)

Example 2: Traditional Maresha

A farmer is ploughing the field using traditional Maresha (plow) pulled by two oxen (Figure 2.24a). The two animals are pulling the beam (Mofer) with a force \(\vec{F}_2\) of 1200 N at an angle of 40° from the horizontal and the farmer’s force \(\vec{F}_1\) on the handle (Erf) is 150 N at 60° above the horizontal as shown in Figure 2.24b. Find the resultant horizontal pulling force exerted by the farmer and the oxen on the Maresha? At this point assume all the forces are acting at point K.

For the whole system to work the resultant downward vertical force must be slightly greater than the upward. Explain why.

Solution

The forces that are acting on the Maresha are the force exerted by the farmer on the Erf, \(\vec{F}_1\), and the pulling force on the Mofer, \(\vec{F}_2\).

Both \(\vec{F}_1\) and \(\vec{F}_2\) have x components and the sum of these components gives the resultant horizontal pulling force. Both \(F_{1x}\) and \(F_{2x}\) are along the positive x direction.

\(F_x = F_{1x} + F_{2x} = F_1 \cos \theta_1 + F_2 \cos \theta_2\)

\(= 150\text{N} \cos 60^\circ + 1200\text{N} \cos 40^\circ\)

\(= 75\text{N} + 919\text{N} = 994.3 \text{N}\)

If the upward forces on the maresha exceed the downward forces (which is the sum of weight of the plow and the downward component of the force exerted on the Erf), the maresha will be lifted above the ground and ploughing will not be possible.

2.3.3.6 Multiplying or Dividing a Vector by a Number or Scalar

A vector quantity, such as a force, can be multiplied by a scalar quantity (an ordinary number). Multiplying a vector \(\vec{A}\) by a scalar \(c\) results in a new vector \(c\vec{A}\) with a magnitude equal to \(|c|A\) (the absolute value of \(c\) multiplied by the magnitude of vector \(\vec{A}\)).

  • If \(c\) is positive, \(\vec{A}\) and \(c\vec{A}\) are in the same direction (they are parallel).
  • If \(c\) is negative, \(\vec{A}\) and \(c\vec{A}\) are in opposite directions (they are antiparallel).

For instance, if vector \(\vec{A}\) is multiplied by 2, the result \(2\vec{A}\) is a vector with twice the magnitude of \(\vec{A}\) pointing in the same direction. If \(\vec{A}\) is multiplied by -2, the result \(-2\vec{A}\) is a vector with twice the magnitude of \(\vec{A}\) pointing in the opposite direction.

Example 1: Scaling a Displacement Vector

A displacement vector is given as \(\vec{S} = 35 \, \text{km}\), toward 60° North of East. Find the magnitude and direction of:

a) \(1.2\vec{S}\)

b) \(-1.2\vec{S}\)

Solution

Given \(\vec{S} = 35 \, \text{km}\), toward 60° North of East.

a) For \(1.2\vec{S}\): This is a displacement vector with a magnitude 1.2 times that of \(\vec{S}\) and in the same direction as \(\vec{S}\).

Magnitude: \(1.2 \times 35 \, \text{km} = 42 \, \text{km}\)

Direction: 60° North of East

So, \(1.2\vec{S} = 42 \, \text{km}\), 60° North of East.

b) For \(-1.2\vec{S}\): This is a displacement vector with a magnitude 1.2 times that of \(\vec{S}\) but in the opposite direction to \(\vec{S}\).

Magnitude: \(1.2 \times 35 \, \text{km} = 42 \, \text{km}\)

Direction: The opposite of 60° North of East is 60° South of West.

So, \(-1.2\vec{S} = 42 \, \text{km}\), 60° South of West.

2.3.3.7 Dot Product

The scalar product of two vectors is denoted by \(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B}\) and is also called the dot product. Although \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\) are vectors, the quantity \(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B}\) is a scalar (a number without direction).

The dot product of vectors \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\) is defined as:

\(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = AB \cos \theta\)

Where \(\theta\) is the angle between the vectors when they are placed tail-to-tail, and it ranges from 0° to 180°.

Depending on the value of the cosine of the angle between the vectors, the scalar product \(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B}\) may be positive, negative, or zero.

The dot product can also be expressed in terms of the component form of the vectors:

If \(\vec{A} = A_x \hat{i} + A_y \hat{j}\) and \(\vec{B} = B_x \hat{i} + B_y \hat{j}\), then:

\(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = A_x B_x + A_y B_y\)

Key Terms

  • Scalar (dot) product: A product of vectors that yields a scalar quantity.
  • Angle between vectors: The angle formed when two vectors are placed tail-to-tail.

Example 1: Scalar Product and Angle

Vector \(\vec{A} = \hat{i} + \hat{j}\) and vector \(\vec{B} = -2\hat{i} + 3\hat{j}\).

What is (a) the scalar product of \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\)?

What is (b) the angle between \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\)?

Solution

a) The scalar product \(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B}\):

Given \(\vec{A} = 1\hat{i} + 1\hat{j}\) (so \(A_x = 1, A_y = 1\))

Given \(\vec{B} = -2\hat{i} + 3\hat{j}\) (so \(B_x = -2, B_y = 3\))

\(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = A_x B_x + A_y B_y = (1)(-2) + (1)(3)\)

\(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = -2 + 3 = 1\)

b) The angle between \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\):

We use the formula \(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = AB \cos \theta\), so \(\cos \theta = \frac{\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B}}{AB}\).

First, find the magnitudes of \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\):

\(A = \sqrt{A_x^2 + A_y^2} = \sqrt{1^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{1 + 1} = \sqrt{2}\)

\(B = \sqrt{B_x^2 + B_y^2} = \sqrt{(-2)^2 + (3)^2} = \sqrt{4 + 9}\) \(= \sqrt{13}\)

Now, calculate \(\cos \theta\):

\(\cos \theta = \frac{1}{(\sqrt{2})(\sqrt{13})} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{26}}\)

\(\cos \theta = \frac{1}{5.099} \approx 0.196\)

\(\theta = \cos^{-1}(0.196) \approx 78.7^\circ\)

Example 2: Work Done by a Force (Dot Product Application)

Consider a block placed on a horizontal surface. A force \(\vec{F}\) is applied to move the block through a displacement \(\vec{S}\). The work done (\(W\)) by the force is defined as the dot product of the force and the displacement.

If \(\vec{F} = (5\hat{i} + 3\hat{j})\) N and \(\vec{S} = (-2\hat{i} + 4\hat{j})\) m, what is the work done by the applied force?

Solution

Work done \(W = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{S}\)

\(W = (5\hat{i} + 3\hat{j}) \cdot (-2\hat{i} + 4\hat{j})\)

\(W = (5)(-2) + (3)(4)\)

\(W = -10 + 12\)

\(W = 2 \, \text{Nm}\)

\(W = 2 \, \text{J}\)

The work done by the applied force is 2 Joules.

Example 3: Power Developed by a Machine (Dot Product Application)

Consider a force \(\vec{F}\) of a certain machine applied on a body to move the body with an average velocity \(\vec{V}\). The power developed by the machine is defined as the dot product of the force and the average velocity.

If \(\vec{F} = (50\hat{i} + 30\hat{j})\) N and \(\vec{V} = (3\hat{i} + 4\hat{j})\) m/s, what is the power developed by the machine?

Solution

Power \(P = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{V}\)

\(P = (50\hat{i} + 30\hat{j}) \cdot (3\hat{i} + 4\hat{j})\)

\(P = (50)(3) + (30)(4)\)

\(P = 150 + 120\)

\(P = 270 \, \text{Nm/s}\)

\(P = 270 \, \text{W}\)

The power developed by the machine is 270 Watts.

Unit Summary

  • A scale is a set of numbers, amounts, etc., used to measure or compare the level of something.
  • There are four types of measurement scales: nominal, ordinal, interval, and ratio. In physics, most scales are ratio scales.
  • Measurement is the comparison of an unknown quantity with a known one (standard unit).
  • Standard units are conventional units used to measure physical quantities scientifically.
  • Traditional measuring units are often not exact and lack a standard.
  • Prefixes are used to simplify the description of very large or very small physical quantities.
  • Physical quantities are characteristics or properties of an object that can be measured or calculated from other measurements.
  • Physical quantities are classified as fundamental/basic and derived quantities.
  • Length, time, mass, temperature, current, amount of substance, and luminous intensity are the seven fundamental quantities. All other physical quantities are derived.
  • Meter, second, kilogram, Kelvin, Ampere, mole, and candela are fundamental (basic) units.
  • Physical quantities can be categorized as vectors or scalars.
  • Meter, kilogram, and second are the SI units of length, mass, and time, respectively.
  • SI units can be converted to non-SI units and vice versa.
  • A scalar is a quantity defined only by its magnitude.
  • A vector is a quantity defined by both its magnitude and direction.
  • A vector is represented by an arrow drawn to a suitable scale, where:
    • The arrow's length represents the vector's magnitude.
    • The arrow's head represents the vector's direction.
  • Vector addition is about finding the resultant of several vectors acting on a body.
  • Vector subtraction is equivalent to adding the negative of a vector.
  • The sum of two or more vectors is called the resultant.
  • The resultant of two vectors can be found using the parallelogram method or the triangle method. For more than two vectors, the polygon method is used.
  • The process of finding a vector's components is called resolving a vector.
  • When a vector \(\vec{A}\) is decomposed into its rectangular coordinate system components, the horizontal component is \(A_x = |\vec{A}|\cos \theta\) and the vertical component is \(A_y = |\vec{A}|\sin \theta\).
  • A scalar quantity is described by a single number (magnitude), while a vector quantity is described by both magnitude and direction.
  • Vectors can be categorized as collinear, coplanar, parallel, antiparallel, equal, null, unit vectors, etc., based on their direction, magnitude, and orientation.
  • The graphical method of vector addition uses translation of vectors.
    • Triangle Law: Vectors are connected head to tail to form a triangle; the resultant connects the tail of the first to the head of the last.
    • Parallelogram Law: Vectors are connected tail to tail, and the resultant is the diagonal drawn from their common tail.
    • Polygon Law: Used for adding more than two vectors by joining them head to tail; the resultant connects the tail of the first to the head of the final vector.
  • Analytic methods of vector addition/subtraction are precise and do not require drawing.
  • The resultant of two vectors in the same direction has a magnitude equal to the sum of their individual magnitudes and is directed along that common direction.
  • The resultant of two vectors at right angles has a magnitude equal to the square root of the sum of the squares of their magnitudes. Its direction is determined using trigonometric functions relative to a reference line
  • A unit vector along the x-axis is denoted by \(\hat{i}\) and along the y-axis by \(\hat{j}\). A vector can be represented in terms of its components as \(\vec{A} = A_x \hat{i} + A_y \hat{j}\).
  • If \(\vec{R}\) is the resultant of \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\), then \(\vec{R} = R_x \hat{i} + R_y \hat{j}\), where \(R_x = A_x + B_x\) and \(R_y = A_y + B_y\). The angle that \(\vec{R}\) makes with the x-axis is \(\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{R_y}{R_x}\right)\).
  • The scalar product of two vectors \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\) is defined as \(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = AB \cos \theta\) and can also be calculated using components as \(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = A_x B_x + A_y B_y\).
  • The scalar product of two vectors can be used to determine the angle between the vectors and can also be used to determine the component of one of the vectors in the direction of the other.
